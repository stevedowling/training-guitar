# 1 · The coordination problem - it's automation, not talent

> **You'll learn:** why singing over your own playing falls apart - the same reason your piano hands fight - and the bandwidth model that tells you exactly what to fix.

## Why this matters

You've tried singing while strumming and felt the wheels come off: the strum stutters when the melody moves, or the voice snaps onto the strum's rhythm, or both collapse at the chord change. It feels like a missing gift. It isn't - it's a *bandwidth* problem with a known solution, and diagnosing it properly is most of solving it. This module is the campfire track's foundation, and this lesson is its physics.

## The big picture

Attention is one spotlight, not two:

```text
YOUR ATTENTION (one spotlight, fixed size)
├── guitar part: chords, changes, strum pattern     ← if this needs the spotlight...
├── vocal: melody, words, breathing, pitch          ← ...and this needs the spotlight...
└── keeping them aligned in time                    ← ...this gets nothing, and the crash happens here
```

Two tasks that each need the spotlight cannot run together - that's not a guitar fact, it's a cognition fact (it's also exactly the piano two-hands problem, and the driving-while-talking problem). The escape is not splitting attention - nobody can - it's making one task need *almost none*:

> **You don't learn to do two things at once. You automate one so completely that it runs in the dark, then give the spotlight to the other.**

For guitar-and-voice, the automated one is always the guitar: strumming is rhythmic and repetitive (automatable), melody-with-words is expressive and variable (spotlight-hungry). Every campfire player you've ever envied is running an autopilot right hand; the singing gets the whole spotlight.

## Measuring automation: the talk test

"I know this part" and "this part is automatic" are different claims, and the gap between them is where every attempt has died. The honest instrument:

| Test | Pass condition | What passing means |
|---|---|---|
| **Talk test** | strum the part while answering real questions aloud (recruit anyone; the drummer will *relish* interrogating you) - pattern doesn't stutter | part runs without the spotlight - ready for a vocal |
| Count test (easier) | strum while counting backwards from 50 by threes | halfway there - keep looping |
| Watch test (easiest) | strum while watching TV, pattern survives | automation beginning |

Any part that fails the talk test *will* fail under a vocal - the vocal is just a harder conversation. This test is the module's gatekeeper: nothing proceeds to singing until the guitar under it passes.

## Simplify until automatable

The second insight: automation speed depends on *simplicity*, so the fastest route to singing a song is to strip its guitar part to an anchor version - and anchor versions are what campfire accompaniment *is* anyway:

- **One strum shape**: down, down-up, down, down-up (the universal folk pattern) or even all downs on the beat - the same pattern for every bar of every chord, no fills, no variations.
- **Two chords**: the whole reason *A Horse with No Name* (Em and D6add9/F#... which is two easy shapes a finger-wiggle apart) is this module's chosen song - the left hand's job barely exists, so automation is days, not months.
- **Fancy comes later, and honestly, barely**: listen closely to any beloved campfire performance - the guitar is *simple*. The complexity budget goes to the voice, where the song actually lives for listeners.

> [!TIP]
> This module lives in your *daytime* slots - singing needs noise freedom (the headphone rig can't help the voice). Night sessions keep the electric track moving in parallel; the practice-week plan from module 1 anticipated exactly this split.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: what automation physically is (and the piano-hands connection)</summary>

Cognitive science distinguishes *controlled* processing (spotlight: slow, serial, effortful - one at a time) from *automatic* (runs without attention: fast, parallel-friendly). Skills migrate from controlled to automatic through repetition - literally changing where the brain executes them (early learning lights up prefrontal control regions; automated skill runs largely from motor/cerebellar circuits). The migration is the practice; there is no shortcut, but there's also no gift required - the same loops automate for everyone at roughly rep-count rates. The piano parallel is exact: two independent hands are two controlled tasks; pianists solve it the same way (automate the left-hand pattern until it survives conversation, then the right hand gets the spotlight) - so the ladder you're about to climb in lesson 2 transfers straight back to the piano when you want it. One more encouraging fact: you *already have* dozens of automated guitar behaviours (you don't think about where fret 5 is), proving the machinery works fine in you - it's just never been pointed at strum-under-voice.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - the audit and the anchor

Daytime slot, ~20 minutes, no singing yet (the discipline of *not* singing this week is real):

1. **Test your current state honestly**: pick any song you can strum, run the talk test (or count test if alone). Note where it stutters - chord changes? pattern variations? This is the baseline; write it down.
2. **Learn the anchor pattern** if it isn't already yours: down, down-up, down, down-up, one bar, one chord - metronome at a lazy 70. It will feel insultingly simple. That's the design.
3. **Load Horse with No Name**: the two shapes (standard Em; and the D6/9 shape - fingers 1 and 2 on strings 3 and 1 at fret 2, or any easy fingering a chord chart shows), anchor pattern, switch every bar. Loop it. This loop is the whole guitar part of a real song people love singing - let that sink in as motivation.
4. **Begin the automation reps**: three 5-minute loop blocks this week (they slot into daytime sessions; even TV time works - that's the watch test doubling as practice). End the week with a talk test on the loop and log the result honestly - pass, or which question broke it.
5. Meanwhile, *listen* to the song daily (commute counts): melody, where lines start relative to the bar, where breaths go. The voice's homework is listening this week; its turn at the spotlight comes next lesson.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the anchor pattern itself stutters at the chord change</summary>

The universal fix: keep the right hand moving *no matter what the left hand is doing* - even if the change lands late and a chord is half-formed for a strum. The right hand's steadiness is the automation target; left-hand cleanliness catches up on its own within days. (Stopping the strum to wait for the chord is the habit that's been blocking you for years - this is where it dies.)

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

A written baseline of where the talk test currently breaks; the Horse loop running smoothly; at least one talk test on it logged (pass or a named failure point); and a week of listening in the ears. No singing yet - and if skipping ahead tempted you and you resisted, the module is working.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Explain your piano two-hands problem using the spotlight model - and say which hand should get automated first, by this lesson's logic.
2. Why does the talk test predict singing failure better than "I can play it perfectly" does?
3. A player wants to sing over a fancy fingerpicked part they can *just about* play. What does the bandwidth model predict, and what's the prescription?
4. From the deep dive: what changes in the brain as a part automates, and why is that encouraging for someone who's "never been able to do this"?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Two hands = two controlled tasks fighting for one spotlight; the crash is the coordination layer getting nothing. Automate the *left* (accompaniment patterns - rhythmic, repetitive, the guitar-strum analogue) so the right (melody) gets the spotlight.
2. "Play it perfectly" can be true *with* the full spotlight - it says nothing about spotlight-free running. The talk test occupies the spotlight and reveals whether the part survives, which is the actual condition singing imposes.
3. Crash at the first expressive vocal moment - "just about" means fully controlled processing. Prescription: strip to an anchor version and automate that, or park the fancy arrangement for instrumental use.
4. Execution migrates from prefrontal (controlled) to motor circuits (automatic) with repetition - at ordinary rep rates, in everyone. The inability was never a missing gift; it was un-run reps, which is purchasable with three 5-minute loops a week.

</details>
