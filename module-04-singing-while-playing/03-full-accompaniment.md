# 3 · Full accompaniment - your first sung-and-played song

> **You'll learn:** to extend the climbed verse into the whole song and finish it sung - start to finish, voice and guitar, recorded - then bank the reusable process that makes every future sing-along cheaper.

## Why this matters

A sung verse over a loop is a proof of concept; a *song* - intro, all verses, choruses, an ending, sung and played without stopping - is a campfire contribution. This lesson finishes Horse with No Name as a complete accompaniment performance, and in doing so runs your first full merge of the two course tracks: the finishing method from the electric side, the ladder from this one. Everything after this is repertoire expansion.

## The big picture

The full-song plan is the module-1 method with one ladder-shaped amendment:

```text
CHART the song  →  automate guitar FULL-SONG  →  LADDER each section  →  CHAIN sung joins  →  PERFORM, never stop
                   (not just the loop:              (verses fast now -       (the new join type:     (three recorded runs,
                    intro, ending, dynamics)         the climb repeats        singing ACROSS          voice and all)
                                                     cheaply)                 section boundaries)
```

Horse is nearly all one loop, which is why it was chosen - the full-song guitar automation is mostly *already done*. What's genuinely new: sung joins, vocal stamina, and the performance layer (dynamics, an ending, starting yourself with a count-in you also have to sing after).

## The new work, honestly

**Sung joins.** Instrumental joins were two bars of fingers; sung joins add the voice's own transition - the last line of a verse into the first of a chorus, where a breath must happen *somewhere* and the melody often jumps. Chain them exactly like module 1 joins: last vocal phrase + first vocal phrase, looped over the running guitar. Mark breath points on the chart with a ' (apostrophe - the standard choir notation); deciding breaths in advance beats gasping opportunistically, and it's the difference listeners can't name but absolutely hear.

**Vocal stamina.** Three-plus minutes of singing is physical work nobody warns hobbyists about: hydrate, don't whisper (quiet ≠ breathy - support the sound even at low volume), and expect the last verse to sag *pitch-wise* when tired, exactly like attention sagged in verse 3 back in module 2. Same fix: it's a full-run skill; runs build it.

**Dynamics - the accompanist's whole art.** With one guitar and one voice, arrangement equals dynamics: verses quieter and sparser (lighter strums, maybe thumb-muted), choruses fuller (all six strings, freer wrist). Practice loud/quiet as its own 5-minute drill - the Loud/Quiet game from the duo sessions, played solo with your own voice as the leader.

**The sung count-in problem** - small, real, and nobody mentions it: you count "1-2-3-4", play the intro, and then must *find your starting pitch* from nowhere. The campfire fix: let the guitar hand it to you - the melody's first note is hiding in the chord you're strumming (Horse starts comfortably inside Em); practice plucking the top of the chord and matching it once, then trust it. (Module 5 formalizes this as key-checking; for now, one song, one pitch, one habit.)

> [!TIP]
> Record runs *voice-forward*: phone closer to your mouth than the guitar. Every hobbyist's first accompaniment recordings bury the voice and flatter the strum, which hides exactly the flaws worth hearing. Brave beats flattering; the campfire has no mixing desk either.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: what "finished" means when a voice is involved</summary>

Clean-enough needs recalibrating for singing, because voices - especially returning-after-years voices - improve on a slower curve than hands, and perfectionism about pitch has ended more campfire careers than any actual pitch problem. The standard that matters is *communicative*: words clear, melody recognisable, time steady, delivery committed. Committed is the load-bearing one - a slightly flat note sung with intent reads as style; the same note sung apologetically reads as error (audiences take their cue from the performer's own verdict, which is module-1's never-stop psychology wearing a microphone). So the finish ritual's three recorded runs stand, judged by: would people around a fire sing along with this? Not: would a producer keep it? Pitch precision grows with exactly the mileage this repertoire provides - every sung song in the weekly set is now also a free voice lesson, which is the quiet compounding gift of the campfire track.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - finish the song

Daytime slots; one to two weeks is typical:

1. **Complete the chart** for the whole song: sections, the (gentle) dynamic plan per section, breath marks on every sung line, `COUNT:` and `END:` (a slowing final line with a let-ring chord suits Horse; decide, don't improvise). Pitch-from-chord noted at the top.
2. **Ladder the remaining sections** - the chorus ("la la" heavy: rungs 2-3 collapse into one pass) and verses two and three (mostly rung 2 work - new words, same melody). Pencil rung numbers; expect this to move three times faster than verse one did. Notice that speed: it's the automation from lesson 1 compounding, and it's permanent.
3. **Chain every sung join**, breath marks included. The verse→chorus join gets most reps - it's where the energy shifts and the breath is tightest.
4. **Runs**: full song, voice and guitar, never stop - flubbed words get sung *through* with committed nonsense (a time-honoured folk tradition, truly). Then the finish ritual, all five steps: three consecutive recorded runs (voice-forward), the listen, the caps log entry, the 48-hour audience, into the weekly set.
5. The audience step has a designated victim this time: **the campfire test is a living room** - family, after dinner, "I learned a song". Sing it *to* them, not near them. Log one sentence about how it felt versus how the recording sounds; the gap between those two is stage experience beginning to accumulate.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if full runs keep dying in verse two</summary>

It's almost never the singing - it's the *words* (verse two lyrics are every cover artist's graveyard). Rung 2 the verse away from the guitar entirely: speak it on a walk, in the car, until the words self-retrieve. Word retrieval is a spotlight load like any other; automate it where the guitar can't distract you, then re-merge.

</details>

<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Horse with No Name in the log in caps with `SUNG` attached; three voice-forward recordings; one living-room performance survived; the song in the weekly cold set (sung - the set just got harder and better); and a rung-annotated chart filed as the template for every sing-along to come. The campfire track's foundation is laid - [Module 5](../module-05-the-campfire-set/) builds the set on it.

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. List the three genuinely new work items full-song accompaniment added beyond lesson 2's sung verse - and which existing tool each one reuses.
2. Why do breath marks belong on the chart rather than left to instinct?
3. From the deep dive: define the finished standard for a sung song in one question - and explain why "committed" outranks "accurate" for an audience.
4. Your first living-room run: hands fine, voice fine, but you sang the whole thing *at* the wall behind the family. What skill just revealed itself as unpracticed, and where does the course train it?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Sung joins (chained with the module-1 join method), vocal stamina (built by full runs, like verse-3 attention was), and dynamics/performance layer (the Loud/Quiet game, solo edition, plus charted endings from module 3).
2. Opportunistic gasping lands mid-phrase under pressure exactly when the spotlight is busiest - pre-decided breaths are one less controlled process, and they're audible as polish even though no listener can say why.
3. "Would people around a fire sing along with this?" - because audiences take their verdict from the performer's: committed delivery frames imperfections as style; apology frames them as failure. Confidence is *information* to a listener.
4. Performing to people rather than executing near them - eye contact, address, invitation. Module 5's leading-the-singing lesson trains it, and the capstone gigs test it.

</details>
