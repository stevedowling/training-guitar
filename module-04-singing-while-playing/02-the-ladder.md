# 2 · The ladder: play → talk → hum → sing

> **You'll learn:** the four-rung progression that adds a voice to an automated guitar part without the wheels coming off - climbed on a two-chord song, one phrase at a time.

## Why this matters

Lesson 1 automated the guitar; the mistake now would be jumping straight to full-voiced singing - the leap from "no vocal" to "melody + lyrics + breathing + pitch" is four new loads landing on the spotlight at once, and it re-crashes exactly like the old attempts. The ladder adds one load per rung. Nobody falls off a ladder they climb one rung at a time; people fall off ladders they leap up.

## The big picture

```text
RUNG 4: SING      full words, full melody          ← the goal
RUNG 3: HUM       melody without words             ← adds pitch + phrasing only
RUNG 2: SPEAK     words in rhythm, no melody       ← adds words + timing only
RUNG 1: TALK      conversation over the loop       ← proves the autopilot (lesson 1's test)
────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOUNDATION:       the automated anchor loop
```

Rungs 2 and 3 are the secret - they split "singing" into its two hard components (word-rhythm and melody-pitch) and train each *separately* over the running guitar. Rung 4 then combines two half-familiar loads instead of introducing four raw ones. Total new-thing-at-once count, per rung: one. That's the entire trick, and it's the same one that fixes piano hands.

## Climbing technique, rung by rung

**Rung 1 - Talk (you're here).** The lesson-1 talk test, passed. If it's shaky, stay - everything above rests on it.

**Rung 2 - Speak the lyrics in rhythm.** Chant the verse words over the loop - flat pitch, rap-like, but placed *where the song puts them*. Two aids: your listening week already taught your ear the placement; and anchoring helps - find which word in each line lands on beat 1 (in Horse's first verse, feel how the line starts *just before* the bar and lands its stress early in it) and let those anchor words snap to the strum while the rest flows between. One line at a time, looped, then chain lines - yes, this is chunk-and-chain from module 1, applied to a voice.

**Rung 3 - Hum the melody.** Drop the words; hum (or "da-da") the tune over the loop. Melody without lyrics frees the spotlight from word-retrieval and points it at pitch and phrase shape. Wobbles here are informative: if the *strum* wobbles when the melody leaps, rung 1 wasn't as solid as claimed - drop down without shame; ladders are re-climbable and the second climb is fast.

**Rung 4 - Sing.** Words + melody together, one phrase at a time, chained like everything in this course. The strange experience to expect: it mostly *just works* now - each component arrives pre-automated enough that combining them fits in the spotlight. Where it doesn't, the failure names its own rung (words tangling → rung 2 that phrase; pitch drifting → rung 3 it) and five minutes on that rung repairs it.

> [!TIP]
> Climb *per phrase*, not per song: verse line 1 might be on rung 4 while verse line 3 is still on rung 2 - that's normal and efficient. The chart tracks it: pencil a rung number over each line. (Also: sing quietly at first if self-consciousness taxes the spotlight - volume is a rung of its own for many players, and daytime-empty-house slots exist for a reason.)

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why speaking-in-rhythm is the rung everyone skips (and shouldn't)</summary>

Rung 2 looks silly - grown adults chanting folk lyrics deadpan over a strum - so self-taught players skip straight from humming to singing and hit a mysterious wall: songs where the vocal rhythm is *syncopated* against the strum (which is most good ones; melodies that sit square on the beat sound nursery-rhyme). Speech is how vocal rhythm is learned as pure rhythm: rappers, choir directors, and vocal coaches all use the same tool ("speak the phrase in time before you pitch it") because it isolates the exact skill - placing syllables against a pulse - that separates confident accompaniment from the beat-locked droning that plagues campfire attempts. Bonus discovery on this rung: singing while playing is *rhythmically* just Should I Stay's stops generalized - the voice does things *between* strums, and the subdivision belt (module 3's conveyor of eighth notes) is what both ride on. Players with grid work behind them - you - climb rung 2 noticeably faster. The tracks were never really separate.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - climb Horse, verse one

Daytime slots, ~15 minutes each, three or four this week:

1. **Re-verify rung 1**: talk test on the Horse loop, 60 seconds. Pass → up; wobble → the week starts here and that's fine.
2. **Rung 2, line by line**: verse one is four short lines. Speak line 1 in rhythm over the loop until placement is boring; add line 2; chain. (Lyrics from your licensed source or the countless chord-and-lyric sheets - and remember the anchor-word trick.)
3. **Rung 3**: hum the verse melody over the loop, twice through cleanly. Note honestly whether the strum stayed metronomic - record 30 seconds and check; ears lie under load, phones don't.
4. **Rung 4, phrase-chained**: sing line 1 over the loop. Then 1-2. Then the verse. When the verse survives twice consecutively, log it in caps: `VERSE 1 SUNG OVER LOOP`. That line is the module's summit ridge - the chorus and remaining verses are the same climb with the map already drawn.
5. File a 30-second recording of the sung verse, dated - the campfire track's "before" is lesson 1's baseline note; this is its first "after".

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - the two classic rung-4 failures and their fixes</summary>

*The voice drags the strum*: your strum is following the melody's rhythm - autopilot not yet strong enough for this phrase. Fix: 2 minutes of rung 1 *on exactly these two bars*, then retry. *The strum squares the voice*: you're snapping syllables onto strums, droning. Fix: rung 2 this phrase with exaggerated speech rhythm - almost theatrical - then keep 30% of that exaggeration when you sing. Both failures are information, not verdicts.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

A chart of Horse verse one with rung numbers pencilled and all lines at 4; a dated recording of the sung verse over a steady loop; and - check this explicitly - a strum that a listener would call *unbothered* by the singing. That unbotheredness is the automation showing; it's the whole module in one adjective.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Name the single new load each rung adds, foundation to summit.
2. Your verse line 2 tangles every time at "plants and birds and rocks and things". Which rung does the failure name, and what's the five-minute repair?
3. Why does the ladder work per-phrase rather than per-song - what would per-song climbing cost?
4. From the deep dive: what specific vocal skill does rung 2 isolate, and which module-3 concept turns out to be its foundation?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Rung 1: nothing (proves the autopilot) → rung 2: words-in-time → rung 3: pitch/phrasing → rung 4: the combination (words + melody together).
2. Rung 2 - it's a word-rhythm tangle (that line is a genuine syllable pile-up). Speak it in rhythm over the loop, exaggerated, until boring; re-sing.
3. Phrases fail independently, so per-song climbing holds hostage every easy line to the hardest one - and repairs lose their address ("the song fails" names nothing; "line 3 fails at rung 2" is a prescription).
4. Placing syllables against a pulse as pure rhythm, pitch removed - and it rides the subdivision belt: the eighth-note conveyor from the grid work is what the voice's syncopation pushes against.

</details>
