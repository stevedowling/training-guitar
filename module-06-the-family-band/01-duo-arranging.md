# 1 · Duo arranging - filling the space without a bassist

> **You'll learn:** to arrange rock songs for exactly two instruments - covering the missing bass and second guitar with your right hand, and choosing parts that make a beginner drummer's simplest beat sound like the record.

## Why this matters

Records have bass, rhythm guitar, lead guitar, and layers; your band has one guitar and one nine-year-old. Play the record's guitar part verbatim and the room hears what's *missing*. Arranging - deciding what your one guitar covers, drops, and fakes - is what makes a duo sound intentional instead of incomplete. The good news is historic: guitar-and-drums duos (White Stripes most famously) built entire careers on the exact tricks in this lesson.

## The big picture

The duo's division of labour, once seen, explains every arranging decision:

```text
THE RECORD                  YOUR DUO
bass: low-end + pulse   →   your low strings: power chords, palm-muted roots
rhythm gtr: the belt    →   your right hand: the eighth-note conveyor never stops
lead gtr: hooks/fills   →   the VOCAL riff or a partial - or dropped without shame
drums: the frame        →   her steady beat - simplest version IS the part
vocals: the song        →   you, when the module-4 work is done (optional at first)
```

Your single guitar covers three record instruments *by priority*: low-end first (the duo dies without it), belt second, hooks a distant third. That ranking decides everything below.

## The three coverage moves

**Move 1 - Live low.** Power chords over open-position chords wherever the song allows: an E5 chug at the 7th fret of the A-string owns more sonic floor than a jangly open E. Palm-muted eighth-note roots (Song Two's whole skill) are simultaneously *bass line and rhythm guitar* - the two-for-one that makes duos work. Default hand position for band arrangements: bottom three strings, near the bridge.

**Move 2 - The belt never stops.** In a full band, guitarists breathe (drop out for a bar, let the bass carry). In the duo there is no bass; your silence is *the band's* silence. Arrange out your own rests: where the record's guitar stops, you keep low-string eighths running at 40% volume. Corollary for solos: don't take them (early on) - or "solo" by playing the vocal melody on the low strings while the chug's ghost continues in muted stabs. The rideout of a riff beats a virtuoso hole.

**Move 3 - The riff goes where the singing isn't.** Iconic hooks (the TNT riff, Seven Nation Army's line) are the *identity* of the song - keep them, but notice they almost always live in the gaps between vocal lines. Arrange verse = chug under voice, gap = the hook answers. That call-and-response IS the classic two-piece sound, and it's cheaper to play than it sounds.

## Arranging for the drummer you have

The same priority logic applies to her part, in reverse - *subtract until solid*:

| The record's drums do | Her version 1.0 | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| fills into every chorus | one crash on beat 1 of the chorus | the *arrival* is what the room hears; the fill was decoration |
| hi-hat 16ths, ghost notes | kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4 | that's the frame - everything else was upholstery |
| stops and pickups | full stop, count silently, crash back in | module 3's gap-bar game, now with a job |

The design principle for the whole band: **her simplest solid beat + your low-living belt = a complete sound.** Complexity gets added later, one element at a time, *only* where the current version feels solid - the tempo-ladder rule, applied to an ensemble.

> [!TIP]
> Chart the duo arrangement as a *shared* document: your shorthand line plus a HER line (`V: chug low / HER: kick-snare · CH: full stabs + crash on 1`). She can't read your chart yet - so draw hers: boxes for bars, K and S letters, a star for the crash. A nine-year-old with her own chart holds it like a contract; that's not a metaphor, it's a rehearsal tool arriving next lesson.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: how two-piece bands fake a bassist (the octave illusion)</summary>

The White Stripes' trick, worth knowing even before you need it: an octave pedal (or just EQ - rolling treble off and driving the amp's low mids) makes a guitar's low strings occupy the bass's frequency slot, and the *ear fills in the rest* - psychoacoustics does the hiring. Listeners "hear" a bass in Seven Nation Army's studio riff; there isn't one (it's a guitar through an octave effect). The deeper principle transfers without any pedal: the ear judges a band's fullness almost entirely by (a) low-frequency presence and (b) rhythmic continuity - which are exactly moves 1 and 2. Cover those two and a duo reads as *complete*; miss them and a five-piece reads as thin. If the band gets serious, a cheap octave pedal is the single best-value purchase in two-piece history - but EQ, palm muting, and living low get you 80% of it for free, tonight.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - arrange the band's first two songs

Desk work plus solo reps; the drummer isn't needed until next lesson:

1. **Arrange Seven Nation Army for the duo** (the band already plays it loosely from module 3 - now it gets the real treatment): your chart line per section using the three moves (the riff IS a bass line already - this song is the gentlest possible arranging first date), and a HER line at version 1.0. Draw her chart, boxes and letters.
2. **Arrange her request** - the "what song next?" answer from module 3, on file. Apply the moves honestly; if her pick is arrangement-hostile (it happens - some songs are synth castles), arrange a *cousin*: same feel, duo-friendly, and let her approve the swap. Her buy-in outranks fidelity.
3. **Automate your parts**: both arrangements through the talk test at song tempo - band arrangements need *more* autopilot than solo ones (module 3 taught why: following her costs spotlight). The belt-never-stops bars get specific reps: loop the record's guitar-rest bars with your 40% chug filling them.
4. **The octave-illusion experiment** (10 fun minutes): play the SNA riff open-position jangly, then low, palm-muted, treble rolled off. Record both over a drum loop; listen back. Hearing the "bass" appear teaches move 1 better than any paragraph - and it's a demo she'll want to hear too.
5. File both duo charts in the repo (`band/` directory is born). Next lesson they hit the drum kit.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if her song resists even a cousin-swap</summary>

Extract the *riff* rather than the song: most synth or pop hooks translate to low-string guitar shockingly well (that's half of rock history's business model), and "the band plays the hook of her song as a jam loop" is a legitimate band activity that beats abandoning her pick. The full arrangement can wait for the band's second album, so to speak.

</details>

<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Two duo arrangements charted twice each (yours in shorthand, hers in boxes), both of your parts talk-test solid including the filled rests, one octave-illusion recording that made you grin, and a `band/` directory that just became the band's filing cabinet. Rehearsal-ready.

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Rank the three record instruments your guitar covers, and name the move that handles each.
2. The record's guitar drops out for two bars before the last chorus (a classic tension move). What does the duo arrangement do there, and why is verbatim fidelity wrong for exactly two bars?
3. From the deep dive: what two properties does the ear use to judge a band as "full", and which of your techniques feed each?
4. Her version 1.0 drum part has no fills at all. Defend that choice to an imaginary skeptic in two sentences - one musical, one motivational.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Low-end first (live low: power chords, palm-muted roots), rhythm belt second (the never-stopping eighths), hooks third (call-and-response in the vocal gaps - or dropped).
2. Your 40% muted chug fills them - in the duo, the guitar's dropout is the *band's* dropout, and two bars of near-silence before a chorus reads as a mistake, not tension. (Tension survives via dynamics: 40% then 100% is the same arc, duo-sized.)
3. Low-frequency presence (fed by living low + palm muting + treble roll-off) and rhythmic continuity (fed by the belt never stopping). Two properties, two moves, complete-sounding duo.
4. Musical: the frame (kick 1-3, snare 2-4, crash arrivals) is what a room actually hears as "the drums" - fills are upholstery. Motivational: a part she can play *solidly* tonight makes her sound like a drummer immediately, and sounding good is the only practice motivation that self-renews.

</details>
