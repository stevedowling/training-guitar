# 3 · The duo setlist - three songs tight enough for grandparents

> **You'll learn:** to take the band from "plays songs" to "has a set" - three arrangements tightened with edge work, sequenced, and dress-rehearsed to the point where the living-room gig is just another run-through with applause.

## Why this matters

A band that plays songs is a rehearsal; a band with a *set* is an act. The difference is the same one module 2 taught about songs - finishing - applied one level up: agreed order, rehearsed transitions, edges everywhere, and a definition of done. For this band the stakes are double: the set is also the vehicle that carries her from "learning drums" to "performed in a band" - a sentence that reshapes a kid's identity, and honestly, the guitarist's too.

## The big picture

**The set formula for a beginner duo**: three songs, ~10 minutes, shaped like a tiny arena show:

| Slot | Needs | The likely candidates |
|---|---|---|
| Opener | the band's most solid - confidence for her, proof for the room | Seven Nation Army (the band's oldest friend) |
| Middle | contrast + ownership - different feel, and it's *hers* | her pick, in its duo arrangement |
| Closer | the banger - loud, fun, ends with the big finish | Blitzkrieg Bop or TNT from your module-2 stock |

Same design logic as the campfire set (opener = *performers'* easiest; peak energy at the close for a rock set) - you've now built setlists for both of your musical lives, and the skill is officially portable.

## Tightening: the band edition of edge work

Module 3's edges, upgraded to two players and a show:

**Count-ins are hers, rehearsed as material.** Ten count-in drills into each song's first bar - the band version of the drill you did solo. Her count *at the right tempo* is the show's most load-bearing skill; the hum-first trick transfers (she hums her own drum groove - watching her discover this works is a highlight of the whole course).

**Transitions between songs get charted.** The five seconds after song one's ending is a performance too: applause happens (grandparents are a generous room), she twirls a stick or you say one sentence ("this next one's [her name]'s pick"), her count-in starts song two. Rehearse the *gap* - bands are judged by their silences, and a duo that flows between songs reads as veterans.

**Endings, synchronized.** Each song's charted ending now needs *two* players landing it: the big chord + cymbal choke for SNA, the button + crash for the closer ("ending is ON the 4 - watch me, hit with me"). The visual cue rehearsal from module 3 finally meets its real audience: her eyes on your guitar neck at every ending is the band's secret handshake.

**The full dress run**: setlist order, transitions, endings, bows, *recorded* - the show run-through design from lesson 2, now with everything at stake (nothing at stake). Done when two consecutive dress runs need only the setlist card.

> [!TIP]
> Schedule the gig *before* the set feels ready - two to three weeks out, date on the calendar, grandparents invited by her. A booked show reorganizes rehearsal motivation like nothing else (deadlines are the adult version of her gig excitement, and they work on drummers of every age). The set will rise to the date; sets always do.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: what performing does to a kid's practice curve (and the one risk to manage)</summary>

Performance is the strongest consolidation event in music learning - the pre-gig rehearsal spike, the full-attention run under mild pressure, and the post-gig pride all deepen skills in ways ordinary practice can't (retrieval under stakes is spaced repetition with the gain turned up). For kids specifically, one successful performance typically does more for instrument identity than months of lessons: "I'm a drummer" gets *evidence*, witnessed by the people whose opinion matters most. The risk to manage is equally well-documented: a performance that goes badly *and is treated as going badly* can dent that identity - so the adult's job is rigging the game. Short set, solid parts, generous room, and the never-stop rule rehearsed until automatic (a band that plays through its flubs *has no visible flubs* at grandparent distance). Then the debrief-warm ritual: three specific goods before anything else, and "what should we play next time?" as the closing question - pointing the identity at the future, where it lives.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - build the set, book the show

1. **Complete the third arrangement** (the closer) with lesson-1's moves - her 1.0 part can add ONE new element if her playing has earned it (the crash-on-1 became a fill? her call). Talk-test your part; chart both lines; album folder gets its rehearsal takes.
2. **Set the list**: order per the formula, on a new setlist card - her handwriting again, both charts stapled behind it. The card is the band's second album cover, effectively; ceremony is allowed.
3. **Edge fortnight**: rotate rehearsals through count-in drills (hers), transition runs (the gaps, with patter sentence), and ending synchronization (eyes-on-neck). Log which edge is weakest; give it the next session's game slot.
4. **Book it**: date on the calendar 2-3 weeks out, invitations issued by the drummer personally (phone call to grandparents - autonomy nutrient, maximum dose). Log her verbatim invitation if you can overhear it; module 7 collects these.
5. **Dress runs** until two consecutive card-only successes, recorded. Then stop rehearsing the set - final-week polish is a perfectionism trap at band scale too; keep the standard sessions fun and light until the date. The gig itself belongs to [Module 7](../module-07-capstone-two-setlists/), which takes over from here.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the set plateaus ragged with a week to go</summary>

Shrink the risk, not the show: drop the shakiest song to a "bonus encore if we feel like it" (framing matters - nothing is *cut*), tighten to two songs plus bows, and let the dress runs get boring at the smaller size. A two-song gig that lands beats a three-song gig that wobbles, and encores that happen are legendary; encores that don't are unremembered.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Three duo arrangements (or two plus a bonus) dress-run to card-only twice; a setlist card in her handwriting; every edge - count-ins, transitions, endings - rehearsed as material; a date on the calendar with invitations *issued*; and an album folder documenting the whole climb. The band is gig-ready. See you at the capstone.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Why does the opener slot go to the band's oldest song rather than its best one - and whose nerves is it really for?
2. Chart the five seconds between songs one and two as a sequence of events. Who owns each?
3. From the deep dive: what makes performance a stronger learning event than rehearsal, and what's the one risk the adult must rig against?
4. The band formula and the campfire formula solved the same problem twice. Name two principles they share and one way they differ - the transfer test for everything module 5 and 6 taught.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Oldest = most automated = most nerve-proof, and it's for *both* players' nerves - her first public count-in and your first public following both deserve the material that runs in the dark. (Best-song goes last; rock sets peak at the close.)
2. Ending lands (both, eyes-cue) → applause (the room's job) → stick twirl / one patter sentence (one each, agreed) → her count-in (hers, hum-first) → song two. Every beat owned in advance - that's what "tight" means between songs.
3. Retrieval under mild stakes consolidates deeper (spaced repetition, gain up), and the pride evidence rebuilds identity. The rigged-against risk: a bad show *treated as bad* - managed via short solid sets, the automatic never-stop rule, and the three-goods-first debrief.
4. Shared: opener = performers' easiest (nerves before preferences); edges rehearsed as material (counts, endings, gaps). Differ: energy curve - campfire closes gentle (singable ember), rock closes loud (the banger). Same craft, different rooms - which is the whole point of learning it as craft.

</details>
