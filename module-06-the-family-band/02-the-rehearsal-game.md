# 2 · The rehearsal game - structures that beat "go practice"

> **You'll learn:** to run band rehearsals a nine-year-old asks to repeat - the session designs, the games, and the motivational mechanics that make the band her idea.

## Why this matters

You already know the hard truth: telling her to practice drums is a negotiation that costs more than it yields. This lesson replaces the negotiation with a *pull*: a band with songs, recordings, in-jokes, and an upcoming gig is something a kid opts into - and her drumming improves as a *side effect* of wanting the band to be good. The rehearsal designs here are motivational engineering first, music pedagogy second; both work better that way.

## The big picture

Module 3's ten-minute session shape remains the skeleton. What this lesson adds is the *campaign* around it - the structures that make session #12 still wanted:

```text
THE BAND CAMPAIGN
├── identity      the name, the setlist card with HER charts on it, "band rehearsal" on the calendar
├── repertoire    songs SHE co-owns: her pick is song two, and she has veto votes
├── evidence      every rehearsal recorded; the playback ritual is the last two minutes, always
├── trajectory    a gig on the horizon (module 7's living-room show) - rehearsals AIM somewhere
└── the golden rule: end while it's fun. Every. Single. Time.
```

## Rehearsal designs (rotate; never announce which)

**The standard** (module 3's shape, matured): count-in hers → song one full run → one game → song two full run → playback. Ten minutes.

**The recording session**: one song, three takes, "we pick the best one for the album." The album is a folder in the repo (`band/album/`); it is also completely real to her, and choosing the take *together* - listening critically as peers - is the highest-density musical education this course contains, disguised as admin.

**The show run-through** (once a gig exists): full setlist in order, count-ins, endings, no stopping between songs - a 12-minute dress rehearsal including her bow practice. (Bows are not optional. Bows are half the reason she's in the band.)

**The five-minute special** (low-energy days): one song, once, playback, done. The streak logic from module 1 applies to bands doubly - a tiny rehearsal that ends warm protects the identity; a pushed rehearsal that ends in tears taxes it for weeks.

**The swap** (occasionally, gold): she plays your guitar (power chord, one finger barring is fine), you play her kit (badly - be genuinely bad, it's important). Ten minutes of chaos that pays in empathy both directions and reliably produces the band's best in-jokes.

## The motivational mechanics, named

- **Specific praise only** (module 3's rule, now doctrine): "your crash on the chorus landed exactly with my chord" beats "great job" - it tells her what her skill *is* so she can own it.
- **Never correct mid-song** - the never-stop rule is also a relationship rule. Notes go in the *next* count-in ("this time let's both breathe before the chorus"), framed as the band's note, not hers.
- **She has jobs beyond drumming**: counting in (she's the drummer; it's literally her job), choosing the game, naming the takes ("take 2, the good one"), pressing record. Jobs are ownership; ownership is attendance.
- **You flub visibly and cheerfully.** Your played-through mistakes, narrated after ("I completely missed that change, did you hear it? Kept going though") teach the deepest lesson available: mistakes are weather, not verdicts. She will drum through her own flubs *because she watched you do it*.
- **Scarcity beats nagging**: "band rehearsal is Tuesday and Saturday" (calendar, finite) outperforms "we can practice whenever!" (infinite, therefore never). If she asks for extra sessions - and the design's whole aim is that she does - extra is always yes.

> [!TIP]
> The single highest-leverage two minutes: **playback with her hand on the phone**. Kids who hear themselves improve become self-correcting in a way no instruction achieves - "the chorus was faster than the verse!" discovered by her own ears is worth ten times the same note from you. Your job during playback is to praise one specific thing and otherwise stay quiet; her ears are having a lesson.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: the motivation science under the games (why this works when nagging doesn't)</summary>

Self-determination theory - the most replicated framework in motivation research - says intrinsic drive needs three nutrients: *autonomy* (her picks, her veto, her jobs), *competence* (parts sized to be solid tonight, specific praise, audible progress via playback), and *relatedness* (a band is relatedness incarnate - it's time with you where she's a *colleague*, not a child being managed). Nagging fails because it starves all three at once: it removes autonomy, implies incompetence, and frames the drums as her obligation rather than your shared thing. The band structure feeds all three without a single "you should" - which is why the drummer who won't practice scales will beg for rehearsal #13. One honest caveat from the same research: rewards and pressure *crowd out* intrinsic motivation - so no payment for practicing, no disappointment for skipping, and the gig is an event, never a threat. The band must remain the treat.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - run the campaign's first fortnight

1. **Install the identity layer**: the band name goes on the setlist card (her handwriting), rehearsals go on the family calendar (two a week, ten minutes, finite and defended), and the `band/album/` folder gets created *with her watching* - "this is where our recordings live."
2. **Rehearsal 1 - the standard**, on the two lesson-1 arrangements. Her chart in front of her, her count-in, playback ritual with her thumb on the button. Log: one specific praise given, one thing *her ears* caught at playback.
3. **Rehearsal 2 - the recording session**: SNA, three takes, choose together for the album. Name the take whatever she names it.
4. **Between rehearsals, your homework**: fix whatever following-her cost you last time (the log knows), and prep the next arrangement so the band never stalls waiting for the guitarist. The band's ceiling is your preparation; her motivation is renewable, yours is the infrastructure.
5. **End of fortnight, the trajectory move**: propose the gig - "I think we should play these for the grandparents when we've got three songs. Bows and everything." Log her reaction verbatim; it's the campaign's KPI, and module 7 wants the quote.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if a rehearsal gets refused entirely</summary>

Don't spend the calendar slot - trade it: "no worries, I'll do my practice; want to press record for me?" (a job, not a demand - and watching a parent practice alone is quietly magnetic). Refusals cluster around tiredness and hunger, not the band itself; track the pattern, move the calendar slot, and never let a refusal become a negotiation. Scarcity survives skips; it doesn't survive begging.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Four-ish rehearsals logged across two weeks, at least one *asked for by her*; an album folder with a named take in it; a specific-praise habit that's becoming reflexive; and a gig proposal with her verbatim reaction on file. The band is real - it has a name, a sound, recordings, and a future.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Name the three nutrients from the deep dive and one campaign structure that feeds each.
2. She rushes the chorus in every take of rehearsal 2. Script your exact intervention - timing, framing, words.
3. Why is the playback ritual the highest-leverage two minutes, and what is your only job during it?
4. What's wrong with paying her per rehearsal, per the deep dive - and what's the permitted alternative?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Autonomy: her picks/veto/jobs. Competence: version-1.0 parts plus specific praise plus playback evidence. Relatedness: the band itself - colleague time with a parent. (Any fair mapping counts.)
2. Never mid-song; at the *next* count-in, as the band's note: "let's both watch the chorus this time - I think we get excited together. I'll hold if you hold." Then specific praise the moment it improves even slightly.
3. Self-heard flaws become self-corrections - a mechanism no external note matches. Your job: one specific praise, then silence; her ears are the teacher in that window.
4. External rewards crowd out intrinsic motivation - payment converts the band from her treat into her job, and jobs get quit. Permitted: the gig as an *event*, celebration after milestones, and unlimited enthusiasm - treats that feed the identity instead of purchasing the behaviour.

</details>
