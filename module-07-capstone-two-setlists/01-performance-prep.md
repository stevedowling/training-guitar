# 1 · Performance prep - the run-through week

> **You'll learn:** the final week's craft: setlist logistics, recovery plans per song, and the nerve-management that turns performing from a threat into a slightly heightened run-through.

## Why this matters

Both sets exist; both audiences are booked (the living room) or bookable (the fire). What remains is the discipline every gigging musician learns: the last week is *logistics and nerves*, not more learning - and both yield to preparation. This lesson is deliberately unmusical; it's checklists and psychology, because that's honestly what the week before a show is made of.

## The big picture

```text
THE RUN-THROUGH WEEK (either gig; run it twice, once per set)
├── Day 1-2   full dress runs, performance conditions (standing, card-only, recorded)
├── Day 3     the RECOVERY REHEARSAL - deliberately sabotaged runs (below)
├── Day 4-5   light: one dress run max, otherwise normal fun sessions
├── Day -1    ONE relaxed run, gear check, early night - no cramming (it steals tomorrow's spotlight)
└── Gig day   kit by the door, one warm-up song (not from the set), go
```

The shape's logic: peak the reps early, *taper* late. Cramming the final 48 hours trades marginal polish for accumulated tension - the worst deal in performance. Athletes taper; so do bands.

## Recovery plans: rehearsing the mistakes

The never-stop rule has carried you since module 1; the capstone upgrade is *pre-planning the rejoins*. For each song on both cards, write one line: the **landmark map** - where you rejoin from any collapse:

```text
SNA: riff restarts every 2 bars - rejoin next riff cycle, always
HORSE: chorus every 8th bar - worst case, strum Em till the chorus comes round
STAND BY ME: the 1-6m-4-5 wheel never stops turning - re-board at the next 1
```

Then the **recovery rehearsal** (day 3, the week's strangest and most valuable session): run each song and *sabotage yourself once per run* - deliberately flub a change, skip a line, let the capo sit wrong for a bar - and practice the rejoin cold. For the duo set, sabotage is a game she'll adore ("this run, one of us makes a secret mistake - other one has to keep going"). Rehearsed recovery converts stage fright's core fantasy ("what if it goes wrong?") into a known procedure ("then I rejoin at the landmark, like Tuesday").

## Nerves: the honest briefing

Performance nerves are arousal, not damage - the same adrenaline that sharpens attention also speeds tempos and stiffens hands, and the counter-moves are mechanical:

| Symptom | Counter |
|---|---|
| racing count-ins | the hum-first ritual, *twice*, before song one (your hands will vouch for it by now) |
| stiff strumming hand | the module-2 shake-out + deliberately playing song one at 90% energy - the room can't tell; your wrist can |
| dry mouth / shaky voice | water at your feet (fire kit already says so), and the first sung line *quieter* than rehearsed - control reads as confidence |
| mind blanks on lyrics | the card is *at your feet on purpose* - glancing is professional; only apologizing for glancing is amateur |

And the reframe that outranks technique, collected from both tracks: the living room wants the band to succeed (grandparents are the most generous audience in live music), and the fire is an oxytocin machine you're operating, not an exam. Both rooms were chosen - by this course, on purpose - for maximum warmth per unit of courage.

> [!TIP]
> Write the *patter* on the setlist cards too - the one sentence per gap, plus an opener line ("we're [band name], this is our first show" earns applause before a note lands). Deciding words in advance frees the spotlight for everything else, which sentence you have now read, in various clothes, approximately eleven times this course. It keeps being true.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why mild nerves improve performance (Yerkes-Dodson, briefly)</summary>

The century-old and repeatedly confirmed Yerkes-Dodson curve: performance rises with arousal to a peak, then falls - meaning the goal was never zero nerves (flat, careless runs live *left* of the peak) but the manageable middle, where attention is bright and hands still loose. Everything in this lesson is peak-positioning: automation lowers the load (so the same arousal buys more attention), recovery plans cap the downside fantasies (which is what pushes arousal past the peak), and the taper prevents stacking practice fatigue under gig adrenaline. Veterans aren't calm - ask any of them - they've just rehearsed being nervous until the state itself is familiar. Your dress runs under performance conditions are exactly that rehearsal: the strangeness of standing, card-only, recorded, mattering - met in advance, weekly, until it's Tuesday.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - run the week (×2)

1. **Write the landmark maps** - one rejoin line per song, both cards. Ten minutes, permanent nerve equipment.
2. **Run the week's shape for the duo gig first** (it's booked): dress runs early, the sabotage game day 3 (log her best recovery - there will be one worth quoting), taper honestly, gear staged the night before (amp, cables, cards, her chart, phone-on-tripod for the recording).
3. **Then the campfire's week**, or a compressed version if the fire is opportunistic (family camping trips being what they are): the fire kit lives ready, the landmark maps live in the case, and one recovery rehearsal on the four songs counts as prepared. Campfires forgive; the kit means you're never caught unable to say yes.
4. **Pre-gig rituals, written once**: your two-song warm-up (one electric, one sung - neither from the sets), the hum-first double-check, the water bottle. Ritual is portable calm.
5. Confirm the recording plan for both gigs - module 7's remaining lessons need the tapes, and so does the album folder, and so, someday, does she.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the taper feels like negligence</summary>

That itch is the perfectionism trap wearing a responsible costume (its third appearance: clean-enough, final-week polish, now this). Trust the evidence instead of the itch: two consecutive card-only dress runs was the *done* criterion, and it was met. The taper isn't absence of preparation - it's preparation of the *nervous system*, which performs best rested and slightly bored.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Two landmark-mapped setlist cards with patter lines; a completed run-through week for the duo gig (sabotage game played, gear staged); the campfire kit in permanent yes-ready state; and rituals written down. Nothing left but the gigs themselves - which is the next lesson, and the whole point.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Why does the week peak early and taper late - what does each half protect?
2. Your mind blanks entering Horse's verse two, live. Walk the trained response, landmark map included.
3. From the deep dive: what were the dress runs *actually* rehearsing, beyond the music?
4. Write the one-line landmark map for Blitzkrieg Bop (three chords, relentless) from what you know of it.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Early peak protects the *material* (reps while fresh); late taper protects the *nervous system* (arousal without stacked fatigue). Cramming spends the second to buy scraps of the first - backwards.
2. Strum the anchor pattern on the current chord without breaking time, glance at the card at your feet (professional, unapologetic), rejoin at the landmark - Horse's chorus comes round every 8th bar, and Em till then is musically fine. The room registers a thoughtful instrumental moment.
3. The performance *state* itself - standing, card-only, being recorded, mattering - so gig-day strangeness is familiar territory. Veterans rehearse being nervous, not being calm.
4. Something like: "the progression never varies - rejoin on any bar 1, and the chorus shout is a free re-entry ramp." (Any honest version scores; writing it is the exercise.)

</details>
