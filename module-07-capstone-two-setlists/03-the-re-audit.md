# 3 · The re-audit + what's next

> **You'll learn:** to measure what actually changed - the module-1 audit rerun against the evidence - and to point the system, which is now yours, at the next year of songs.

## Why this matters

Courses end; systems continue. This last lesson does two jobs: it closes the loop opened on day one (the audit file has been waiting months for its rematch), and it hands over the keys - because the difference between "did a course" and "changed how I play" is whether the machinery keeps running unsupervised. It will. Here's the handover.

## The big picture

Open the module-1 audit file next to the album folder and run the comparison honestly:

| Then | Now |
|---|---|
| N fragments, most under 40% of a song | the fragment list, unchanged - *plus* a finished-songs list with dates |
| finishes: probably 0-2 | 7-10: three electric, one sung foundation, four campfire, arranged duo versions |
| "last played a complete song for another human: ______" | two gigs, with recordings, witnesses, and an encore annotation |
| practice = noodling toward whatever glittered | a log with NEXT lines, a weekly cold set, streaks |
| a kid who negotiated about drum practice | a drummer with an album folder and a request list |

Write the new numbers *into the audit file* under the old ones, dated. That document - fragment pile, uncomfortable sentence, and now the rematch - is the course's true certificate.

## The handover: what runs without the course

The system's moving parts, all installed, all yours:

- **The pipeline**: criteria → chart → chunk/chain/perform → ritual → weekly set. Every future song, both tracks, same machine. The checklist card from module 2 is the whole course on an index card.
- **The weekly cold set** - now 20+ minutes and the single non-negotiable. As long as it runs, the repertoire lives and grows; if life collapses everything else, *the set is the five-minute streak-saver's big sibling*: protect it, and the rest regrows.
- **The band campaign** - rehearsals, the album, the debrief culture. Its trajectory device (a gig on the horizon) needs re-arming: book the next one within a month, always. Bands without a next gig dissolve gently; bands with one self-organize.
- **The key cards and number system** - compounding assets. Every song charted makes the next cheaper; somewhere past card twenty, the campfire request game changes from gamble to party trick.

## Pointing it forward: the next twelve

Choose the next songs the way the system taught - and *stagger the tracks* so both stay alive:

1. **The fire's request list** is pre-approved repertoire - harvest it first.
2. **Her next picks** keep the band's autonomy nutrient flowing - the drummer's veto remains constitutional.
3. **One reach song per quarter** - now that finishing is routine, criterion 2 ("at or below the hands") earns a scheduled exception: a song slightly *above* the hands, given a quarter, grows the hands. The system can afford ambition now; it couldn't in module 1, and knowing why is the measure of how far you've come.
4. **The 12-bar door** - the stubs mentioned it twice and it's the natural next chapter: blues form is the improvisation gateway, the easiest jam format for the duo, and the campfire's late-night gear. When it calls, the machinery is ready (chart, chunk, chain - a 12-bar chart takes four minutes).

> [!TIP]
> Re-run the *full* audit annually - same file, new dated section. A practice system's long-term enemy isn't failure but drift, and an annual rematch against written evidence is the cheapest drift-detector there is. (You have a repo. You have version control. The other courses in this collection would be proud.)

<summary>🔍 Deep dive: what actually changed (it wasn't the hands)</summary>

Run the course's own final exam: the hands learned some stamina, a few arrangement moves, tighter placement - real but modest gains, months of ordinary mileage. What actually changed was *infrastructural*: completion became the default (identity: someone who finishes), practice acquired addresses (charts, NEXT lines, phases - the spotlight always knows where to point), performance became familiar territory (the habituation curve, climbed), and motivation got engineered instead of summoned (streaks, campaigns, gigs booked before ready - the next rep always protected). None of it is guitar-specific. It's the general shape of how adults finish things: honest audit, small units with definitions of done, maintenance rituals, stakes scheduled in advance, warmth in the debrief. The piano upstairs already knows its two hands are next; the ladder transfers whole. So does everything else.

## 🛠️ Try it - close the loop

The course's final exercise, mostly ceremonial, entirely earned:

1. **Write the re-audit** into the module-1 file: the table above with your real numbers, dated, plus the rewritten sentence with its new answer.
2. **Draft the next-twelve list**: request-list harvest, her picks, one reach song flagged with its quarter. File as `next-twelve.md` - it's the practice plan's new horizon.
3. **Book the band's next gig** (a month out, any audience - the neighbours count) and float the next fire. The trajectory devices, re-armed.
4. **Tick every module** in the [course home](../README.md) progress list - including this one. You know by now why the ceremony matters: evidence, identity, the next rep.
5. Play the weekly set tonight, all of it, for nobody. Just because you can - which, if you re-read the module-1 audit one last time, was the entire point.
