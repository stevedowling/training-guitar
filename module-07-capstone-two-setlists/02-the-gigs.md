# 2 · The gigs

> **You'll learn:** almost nothing - on purpose. This lesson is the two performances themselves, with just enough structure to protect them, and the debrief craft that converts each into fuel.

## Why this matters

Every module funnelled here: the finishing method exists so there are songs, the edges exist so they start and end, the band campaign exists so there's a drummer bouncing on her stool, the ladder exists so there's a voice over the fire. The gigs are where "I've been learning guitar for years" finally changes tense - and how they're *handled* (before, during, and the twenty minutes after) decides whether they're a summit or a foothill. Foothill is the right answer.

## The big picture

Two performances, two rooms, one rule set:

```text
THE LIVING-ROOM GIG                    THE CAMPFIRE
├── 3 songs + possible encore         ├── 4 songs + the inevitable requests
├── she counts in, she bows           ├── you cue, they sing
├── phone on tripod, recording        ├── phone leaning on a log, recording
├── grandparents: maximum warmth      ├── firelight: maximum forgiveness
└── SHARED RULES: never stop · landmarks live · warmth flows outward · done means celebrated
```

## The living-room gig: run of show

Keep it ceremonial and short - ceremony is what makes it a *gig* rather than "playing in front of people":

1. **Seating, then introduction** - her line or yours, rehearsed: band name, first show, thank you for coming. (Applause point #1 - before a single note. Rooms like being told when to clap.)
2. **The set as dressed**: her count-ins, the transitions as charted, endings eyes-locked. Your job beyond guitar: *visibly enjoying her* - the room takes its cue from your face, and so does she.
3. **Flubs**: played through, per a hundred rehearsals, ideally with the cheerful-flub face she's watched you model since module 3. At grandparent distance, a duo that keeps going has no flubs - this is now a tested law of physics.
4. **Bows.** Practiced, unhurried, together. (Applause point #3; #2 was the middle song, her pick, announced as hers.)
5. **Immediately after**: the band watches nothing, fixes nothing - snacks and glow first. The recording waits for the debrief; the moment belongs to the moment.

## The campfire: run of show

Looser by design - fires reward reading the room over executing the plan:

1. **Start when starting feels natural** - the first song *is* the invitation.
2. **The set as designed**, but promotable and droppable: the room's energy outranks the card. The leading moves - cue face, hold-the-floor, verse retreat - are the actual setlist now.
3. **Requests will come** (the campfire law). The kit answers: play what you know, "hum it" for the four-progressions gamble when brave, and "that one's on the list for next time" as the graceful no. Taking a request seriously - even declined - is leading too.
4. **Land it**: the closer's slowed last line, the ring, the quiet after. Fires end sets better than any stage - let it.

## The debrief: twenty minutes, next day, non-optional

Same protocol both gigs, the one from the band campaign, now formalized as the course's closing ritual:

- **Watch the recording together** (band gig: with her; campfire: with yourself, kindly).
- **Three specific goods first** - named, out loud, before anything else. This isn't softness; it's accuracy - warmth-first debriefs are the ones that get repeated, and repetition is the whole game.
- **One "next time"** - singular, concrete, forward-facing ("next time we'll rehearse the encore too"). Not a list. Lists are for practice logs, not afterglows.
- **The question that seals it**: *"what should we play next time?"* - to her, to yourself, to the fire circle if it's still around. The gig ends pointed at the next one; that's what makes it a foothill.

> [!TIP]
> Save everything: both recordings dated into the album folder, the setlist cards (annotated with what actually happened - "encore: yes, twice"), her invitation quote, the request list from the fire. Module 7's last lesson is built from these artifacts, and in ten years they will be the only possessions from this entire course that matter.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why the first gig is the hardest you'll ever play (a promise, not a warning)</summary>

Performance anxiety follows a steep habituation curve: the physiological response to gig #1 is typically the strongest you will ever experience, with #2 markedly milder and #5 barely registering as an event - the state becomes familiar, and familiar states stop triggering alarm. This is why the course rigged gig #1 so heavily (shortest set, warmest room, most-automated material, rehearsed recovery): not to make it easy, but to make its *memory* good - because the habituation curve only runs if there's a #2, and #2 only happens if #1 is remembered warmly. It's the same design as her drum motivation, the same as your practice streaks: the system optimizes for *the next rep happening*. After these two gigs, the pattern is yours to continue - each one cheaper than the last, each one funding the next. That's not a metaphor for the course; it IS the course, played out at performance scale.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - play them

1. **The living-room gig happens.** Run of show, snacks, glow. Next-day debrief per protocol - her three goods, her "next time", her answer to the closing question, all logged in the album folder.
2. **The campfire happens** - booked or opportunistic. Run of show, requests honoured or gracefully declined, the landing landed. Self-debrief next day, same protocol, written.
3. **Artifact both** into the repo: recordings, annotated cards, quotes, the request list. (The band's album folder now contains a live album. This is objectively excellent.)
4. If either gig *can't* happen soon - schedules, seasons, life - the run-through week's final dress run, performed for a phone with full ceremony, counts as gig #1 and earns the debrief. Recorded ceremony beats indefinite waiting; the real room will come.
5. Then proceed to the final lesson while the glow holds - it's short, and it's the course's actual ending.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if gig #1 goes genuinely sideways</summary>

The protocol holds *hardest* here: three specific goods exist in every performance that reaches its final chord (the recording will prove it - "we never stopped" is always one), the one next-time gets named without ceremony, and the closing question still gets asked. A sideways gig debriefed warmly becomes a war story - bands are half war stories - while a sideways gig catastrophized becomes a last gig. You are the debrief culture now; that's the leader's last job.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Two performances survived and celebrated (or one plus a full-ceremony recorded dress); two warm debriefs on file with next-times and next-song answers; an album folder containing live recordings and annotated setlist cards. The sentence from module 1's audit - "last time I played a complete song for another human" - now has an answer measured in days, with witnesses.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. What is your non-musical job during the living-room gig, and why does it outrank clean changes?
2. A campfire request lands for a song you half-know. Enumerate the kit's three responses and what governs the choice.
3. Recite the debrief protocol from memory - order matters. Why three goods *before* the next-time?
4. From the deep dive: what was gig #1 rigged to optimize for - and where has that design appeared twice before in this course?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Visibly enjoying her - the room reads your face for its verdict, and so does the drummer. A parent grinning through a flubbed change teaches the room (and her) that the band is *fine*; clean changes teach nobody anything.
2. Play it (if it's repertoire), "hum it" (the four-progressions gamble - choose when the room's warm and the stakes feel fun), or the graceful no with a promise. Governed by honest automation levels and the never-stop principle: don't start what you can't land.
3. Watch together → three specific goods → one concrete next-time → "what should we play next time?" Goods first because the afterglow window sets the memory's tone, and the memory's tone decides whether there's a next rep.
4. The next rep happening - memory optimized over performance. Previously: her rehearsal design (end while it's fun, always) and your practice streaks (five-minute sessions that protect the identity). One principle, three scales.

</details>
