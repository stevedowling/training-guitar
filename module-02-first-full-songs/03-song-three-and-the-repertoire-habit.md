# 3 · Song Three + the repertoire habit

> **You'll learn:** to finish a song mostly unsupervised - and to install the maintenance system that keeps finished songs performable forever, which is the difference between a repertoire and a scrapbook.

## Why this matters

Two songs in, the method should be feeling less like instructions and more like *how practice works now*. Song Three tests that: minimal guidance, your own pacing, one new wrinkle (a stop-start arrangement that sets up the duo work ahead). Meanwhile a quiet problem is growing: every finished song adds to the pile of things that can silently rot. The repertoire habit - fifteen minutes a week - is what keeps three songs, and later thirty, ready to play *tonight*.

## The big picture

**Song Three candidates** (or your own, criteria-checked): *Should I Stay or Should I Go* (The Clash) - the stop-start arrangement is a joy and a genuinely new full-song skill (silence you must *count through*, not feel through); *Come As You Are* (Nirvana) - clean, watery riff and a chorus lean, if you want contrast with Song Two's chug; *TNT* - if Blitzkrieg won lesson 2 and the AC/DC itch remains.

**The wrinkle worth choosing Should I Stay for**: full-band stops. The guitar slams silent with everyone else, the vocal floats a line, the band slams back in - and there is no way to fake it. You count the silence ("2, 3, 4, AND") or you're early/late in a way the whole room hears. It's two bars of the hardest rhythm skill in this course, it's why drummers love the song, and module 3 will reuse it as a duo drill. (If you pick another song, add a stop-start drill anyway: mute two bars mid-run on purpose, count re-entry. Cheap insurance.)

## Finishing on your own

The full sequence, compressed to a checklist - this is the whole method on one card, and the card is yours now:

```text
□ criteria check (love / at-or-below / audience)     □ chart: shape, bars, tempo, ⭐ joins
□ NEXT lines written before sessions                 □ chunk: slow, minimum tension, ×3 clean
□ chain: 2+2 → 4+4 → whole, every ⭐                  □ runs: with the recording, NEVER STOP, ▷ on chart
□ finish ritual: 3 consecutive recorded · listen · log in caps · audience ≤48h · into maintenance
```

The self-supervision skill is noticing *which box a stall belongs to* - by now every failure mode you'll meet has appeared once with a name (fog → chart it; wobble at bar X → it's a join; tension timeline → stamina intervals; "not ready" feeling past clean-enough → perfectionism, ship it).

## The repertoire habit

Finished songs decay quietly - not the fingers, the *retrieval*: three weeks unplayed and verse 2's entry needs a think, six weeks and the solo's gone shy. The fix is tiny and non-negotiable:

> **The weekly set: play every finished song, full run, once. No stopping, no fixing, in setlist order, cold.**

Three songs ≈ ten minutes. Rules of the set: it's a *performance*, not practice (never-stop applies); it's played **cold** - no warm-up run first, because the campfire won't offer one; and anything that wobbles gets a note in the log, earning a five-minute chain-repair in the next normal session (not a spiral back into full practice - repertoire repairs are spot-welds, not rebuilds).

The weekly set also becomes the course's best measurement: its *length* is your repertoire, its *cold quality* is your real skill, and the calendar makes both visible. When it hits 25 minutes, congratulations - that's a set, in the gigging sense, and module 7 will treat it as one.

> [!TIP]
> Put the weekly set on a fixed slot (Sunday afternoon suits it - daytime noise freedom, week's end symbolism) and let it be *fun*: this is the part of practice that is literally just playing songs you like, complete, well. The fragment years never once delivered that feeling. Notice it.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: spaced retrieval - why one cold run a week beats daily polishing</summary>

Memory research's most robust finding: retrieval strengthens storage far more than re-study does, and *spaced, slightly-effortful* retrieval strengthens it most. A cold weekly run is a deliberately engineered retrieval event - hard enough to matter (a week's forgetting has begun; "wait, how does the bridge start?" is the feeling of the memory being re-earned), easy enough to succeed. Daily runs of the same song are re-study wearing a costume: smooth, pleasant, and weak, because nothing was ever at risk of being forgotten. This is also why the set is played cold and why wobbles are *good news on schedule* - each one marks a memory caught at the perfect moment: struggling, and therefore about to get stronger. (It's the same science behind the flashcard apps - your setlist is an Anki deck your hands answer.)

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - the unsupervised finish

1. Pick Song Three, run the checklist card top to bottom at your own pace. Target: finished inside three weeks, but the real assignment is *noticing you didn't need the lessons open*.
2. If you chose Should I Stay: give the stops their own chain treatment (loop the 4 bars around each stop; count the silence out loud until counting silently is boring). If you chose another song: the two-bar mute drill, three runs' worth.
3. Install the weekly set: fixed slot, all three songs, cold, in an order you choose (you just made your first setlist decision - openers want confidence, so probably Song One). Log it as `SET: ✓✓~ ` with a wobble note if any.
4. Run the set twice (two weeks) before starting the next module's material, letting one repertoire repair happen properly: wobble noted → five-minute spot-weld → clean next week. Feel the loop close.
5. Update the audit file's header: `finished songs: 3` - then reread the fragment list below it. That's the before/after module 1 promised, and it's real now.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if Song Three drags past week three</summary>

Diagnose with the card, but also consider the honest possibility: post-milestone slump is real (two finishes bought some psychological license to coast). The fix is smaller sessions, not bigger resolve - drop to the 5-minute streak-saver version for a week (one run, one log line) and let momentum re-accumulate. The system bends; it's designed to.

</details>

<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Three songs finished (ritual and all), a weekly set that has run at least twice with one repair cycle completed, a checklist card you barely glanced at by the end, and an audit header reading `finished songs: 3`. Module 2 complete - the electric track continues in [Module 3 · Rhythm Lock-in](../module-03-rhythm-lock-in/), where these songs meet a metronome, and then a drummer.

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Sunday set, Song Two's bridge entry wobbles but you get through. What happens (a) in that moment, (b) in the log, (c) in Tuesday's session - and what does *not* happen?
2. Why is the weekly set played cold, in one phrase from the deep dive?
3. Should I Stay's stops: why can't you "feel" your way through two bars of silence, and what's the professional's tool?
4. A friend hears about the system and asks what it takes to go from years-of-bits to three finished songs. Answer with the checklist card's six lines from memory - then count how many involve new physical technique.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. (a) Never stop - play through, finish the set. (b) `SET: ✓~✓ - S2 bridge entry`. (c) Five minutes of 2+2 chaining on that join, spot-weld only. What doesn't happen: Song Two re-entering full practice rotation - repairs are welds, not rebuilds.
2. Because retrieval at slight difficulty is what strengthens memory - warm runs are re-study, cold runs are the workout.
3. Silence has no groove to ride - feel drifts within a bar; the tool is counting, out loud until it's automatic ("2, 3, 4, AND"), which is why the song is secretly a rhythm lesson.
4. Criteria → chart → NEXT lines → chunk slow → chain joins → run never-stop → ritual → maintain. Zero of them are new physical technique - which was the diagnosis all along: the hands were never the problem.

</details>
