# 2 · Song Two: power chord stamina

> **You'll learn:** to play a full-length power chord song without your hand, wrist, or timing giving out - the difference between knowing the shapes (you do) and owning them for three minutes (nobody's fragment ever taught).

## Why this matters

Song Two is chosen to be a *workout*: a Ramones or AC/DC-style song where the whole piece is driving power chords. There's nothing in it your fingers don't know - which is exactly the point. Fragments never revealed that your right hand tires at ninety seconds, your fretting hand death-grips under load, and your downstrokes drift ahead of the beat when you stop listening. Full songs reveal it; this lesson fixes it. It's also the foundation of the duo: when the band is guitar and drums, *relentless rhythm guitar is the band*.

## The big picture

Pick from the candidates: *Blitzkrieg Bop* (Ramones - three chords, ~176 bpm, pure engine) or *TNT* (AC/DC - slower, spacious, the riff everyone knows). Blitzkrieg is the better workout; TNT the better first duo song. You'll likely do both eventually - chart the one you pick (15 minutes, lesson 2 method; both are gloriously simple charts).

The skill being trained has a name:

> **Stamina = relaxation, repeated.** Fatigue in rhythm playing is almost never weakness - it's *tension*: death-grip fretting, a locked wrist, a clenched shoulder. The fix isn't building muscle; it's noticing and releasing, hundreds of times, until relaxed is the default under load.

## The technique audit (10 minutes, once)

Before chunking, check the three tension sources against a mirror or phone video - years of fragment playing can hide surprisingly fixable habits, because fragments end before habits hurt:

| Check | Test | Fix if failing |
|---|---|---|
| **Fretting grip** | mid-riff, can you lift your thumb off the neck for a beat and keep playing? | you're squeezing far harder than the note needs - practice at the *minimum* pressure that sounds clean, and re-find it every session |
| **Picking wrist** | is the motion from the wrist (small, loose) or the elbow (big, locking)? | wrist leads, elbow follows; imagine shaking water off the hand |
| **Shoulders + breath** | 60 seconds in: are your shoulders up? are you breathing? | drop and exhale on every chorus - literally write BREATHE on the chart |

Palm muting - the *chugging* that powers this genre - has its own micro-audit: the palm edge rests on the strings *at the bridge* (too far forward chokes the pitch), and the mute pressure, like the fret grip, wants the minimum that gives the chug.

## Building the three minutes

Stamina builds like couch-to-5k - extend the clean interval, rest, repeat:

1. **One minute, relaxed** at 70% tempo: loop the main progression, attention entirely on the three tension checks. Stop *before* it degrades. Shake out. Again.
2. **Extend to sections**: full verse + chorus relaxed, then two of each, climbing tempo only when relaxation survives the climb. (Rushing the tempo ladder rebuilds tension with a faster metronome - the check is always "still loose?", never "still possible?")
3. **The downstroke question**: Blitzkrieg is famously all-downstrokes; at 176 that's honest work. Two legitimate paths: build it (it's *the* sound, and the arm adapts in a couple of weeks of interval practice), or alternate-pick the verses and save downstrokes for choruses (nobody at the living-room gig files a complaint). Choose consciously; log the choice.
4. **Full runs, never stop** - and now the run diagnostic from Song One gets a stamina column: note *where* in the timeline tension arrives. It moves later every week; that migration is the progress bar.

> [!TIP]
> The best stamina tool you own is the recording of the actual band - not because it's inspiring (it is) but because real bands *breathe*: verses sit back, choruses lean in. Playing along teaches dynamic variation, and varied effort is sustainable in a way that flat-out isn't. Robots tire; bands cruise.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why downstrokes, and what the Ramones were actually doing</summary>

All-downstroke rhythm at speed isn't machismo - it's a *sound*: every hit lands with the same attack and slight natural accent, producing the relentless, square-shouldered drive that alternate picking (with its lighter upstroke) genuinely can't fake. Metal took the technique from punk and built a genre on it (early Metallica is a downstroke seminar). The physical trick the greats converge on: the stroke is a *fall and recover* - gravity does the down, the muscle only does the small lift back - at which point the arm is pacing itself rather than pushing. When yours works, it will feel suspiciously easy; that's not cheating, that's the technique arriving. And the honest footnote: Johnny Ramone tuned this engine over thousands of hours of full-song, full-set playing - stamina was *the* skill of that band. There is no fragment-sized version of it, which is why Song Two exists.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - finish Song Two

The method is now yours; this lesson only adds the stamina layer:

1. Pick and chart the song (tempo on the chart). Run the 10-minute technique audit on video; log what you found - most players find at least one death-grip.
2. Work the interval ladder into your normal sessions: the 13 work-minutes become tension-checked loops extending toward full sections. Headphone nights are perfect for this - it's repetitive, quiet-friendly work.
3. When runs start, log tension-arrival timestamps per run ("tight at 1:40" → "tight at 2:30" → clean). That column reaching *end of song* is your cue for the finish ritual.
4. Execute the finish ritual - all five steps, no skipping the 48-hour audience. (The drummer should hear this one *loud*, on a daytime slot: it's the most drummer-shaped song you know now, and module 6 will want her already humming it.)
5. Log the Song Two paragraph: what stamina work revealed that years of playing hadn't.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the picking arm just won't relax at tempo</summary>

Drop *volume*, not just tempo: play the loop at half your normal attack. Tension often lives in playing *hard*, and the nervous system relearns loose faster when quiet. Then bring volume back one notch per clean minute. (Also check the strap height if standing - a too-low guitar makes every technique a reach. Yes, it looks cooler. Pick your battles.)

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Ninety seconds into a run your fretting hand aches. What is it almost certainly, what's the mid-run response (there is one), and what's the session-level fix?
2. Why does the tempo ladder gate on "still relaxed?" rather than "still accurate?" - what does climbing on accuracy alone build?
3. Where should your picking palm sit for a chug that stays at pitch, and what does drifting forward do?
4. From the deep dive: what makes all-downstrokes sound different from alternate picking, and what's the physical trick that makes it sustainable?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Death-grip tension. Mid-run: consciously drop to minimum pressure and keep going - the never-stop rule extends to discomfort that's tension (pain-pain stops the session, always). Session fix: minimum-pressure practice and the thumb-lift test until loose is default.
2. Accuracy survives moderate tension; relaxation doesn't - so an accuracy-gated ladder happily builds *fast tension*, which caps out and eventually hurts. Loose-at-every-rung is what compounds.
3. Palm edge at the bridge saddles; drifting toward the neck chokes the strings sharp and kills the pitch of the chug.
4. Uniform attack and accent on every hit - a drive upstrokes dilute. The trick: fall-and-recover, letting gravity play the downstroke and spending muscle only on the lift.

</details>
