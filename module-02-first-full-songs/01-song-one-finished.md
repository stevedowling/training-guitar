# 1 · Song One, finished

> **You'll learn:** how a song actually moves through the finishing method week by week - by taking your easiest candidate all the way to three recorded runs. Your first finish in years happens in this lesson.

## Why this matters

Module 1 built the system; this lesson runs it end to end on a real song, once, with commentary - so every future song has a worn path to follow. The first finish is also the psychological one: it converts the system from a nice theory into a thing that demonstrably works on you.

## The big picture

A typical Song One timeline, three 20-minute sessions a week (yours will vary; the *shape* won't):

| Week | Phase focus | The chart at week's end |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Chunk the unlearned sections | every section ✅, joins still ⭐ |
| 2 | Chain every starred join, first honest full runs | joins cleared, `▷▷` ugly-but-unbroken |
| 3 | Perform: runs with the recording, then the finish ritual | `▷▷▷` clean-enough, three recordings on the phone |

Two to four weeks is normal. Faster usually means the song was too easy to teach anything (fine for Song One!); much slower usually means a criterion-2 violation - the song is above the hands, and swallowing that lesson now is cheap.

## The worked example: Seven Nation Army

Your pick may differ - map the same moves onto it. From the module-1 chart, SNA is: THE RIFF, the chorus stabs, the octave-up solo, and joins.

**Chunking notes.** The riff: everyone plays it; almost nobody plays it *for four minutes with consistent tone*. Chunk it with attention to the slide into the 5th fret and the muting between notes - the recording is remarkably staccato; let the space be part of the riff. The chorus: three power shapes (E5, G5, A5) hit as stabs - the skill is the *silence between stabs* (release pressure, don't lift off). The solo: the riff an octave up - same fingering shape, 12th position; five minutes of chunking, mostly a free gift.

**Chaining notes.** SNA's killer join is verse → chorus: riff groove into stab silence is a *feel* change, not a finger problem - loop the last riff cycle + first two stabs until the gear-change is boring. Chorus → verse (back into the groove) is the other one; solo joins are the same two in disguise.

**Performing notes.** The recording is ~124 bpm and relentless - your first runs will expose that the riff wanders when your attention does (verse 3 is where minds go for a walk; module 1 called it: stamina and attention are full-run skills). Never stop. Rejoin on the next riff cycle - the riff restarts every two bars, so a landmark is never far.

## The finish ritual

When runs are consistently clean-enough, make finishing *an event*, not a fade-out:

1. **Three runs, one sitting, all recorded.** Not best-of-five-attempts - three consecutive intentions. (A flubbed-through note is fine; a stop restarts the count. The never-stop rule has teeth tonight.)
2. **Listen to one full take.** All of it. You will hear flaws; you will also hear, for the first time in years, *yourself playing a complete song*. Both reactions are correct.
3. **Log the finish** with a date and caps: `2026-07-31 · SEVEN NATION ARMY FINISHED ▷▷▷ · first finish since ____`. Fill the blank honestly - it's the audit file's uncomfortable sentence, answered.
4. **Play it for someone within 48 hours.** Family counts. The drummer *especially* counts (this is her future setlist - let her hear where the band is heading). Criterion 3 said songs need destinations; deliver this one.
5. **Schedule its maintenance**: it's now the warm-up run in your standard session, at least weekly.

> [!TIP]
> Resist the urge to keep polishing past clean-enough - Song Two is worth more than Song One's final 5%. The repertoire compounds by *adding songs*, and your polish standard will rise on its own as finishes accumulate. (It's the fragment loop's last trick: perfectionism is noodling with a work ethic.)

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: what the first finish changes, psychologically</summary>

Identity research keeps finding the same loop: evidence changes self-image, and self-image changes behaviour more durably than willpower does. For years the available evidence said "I'm someone who plays bits of things" - and behaviour obliged. One dated recording of one complete song is small, but it's *counter-evidence*, and counter-evidence compounds: the second finish is easier partly because your hands improved but mostly because "someone who finishes songs" is now a supportable claim. This is also why the ritual insists on recording, logging, and an audience - each one turns a private feeling into evidence that can't be quietly dismissed on a bad day. Skip the ceremony and the finish still counts; keep it and the finish *recruits*.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - run the plan

This lesson's exercise is simply the work, structured:

1. Confirm Song One against the three criteria one last time (love it / at-or-below hands / has an audience). Chart on the stand, tempo written on it.
2. Write the next six NEXT lines in your log *now* - a plausible route through chunk and chain (e.g. "chorus stabs slow ×3", "V→C join 2+2", ...). Plans made mid-session get eaten by noodling; you know this.
3. Work the sessions. When runs begin, add the `▷` count to the chart - visible progress is fuel.
4. Execute the finish ritual, all five steps. The 48-hour audience clock starts at run three.
5. Write one paragraph in the log: what the method got right, where it chafed, what you'd tell someone starting Song One. (You're building your own manual - module 7's re-audit reads this back to you.)

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if week 2 stalls</summary>

Stalls are nearly always one join hiding as "the song isn't ready". Find it: do a run, note the exact bar where it wobbles, and give that join its own session. If it's genuinely a *section* (fingers can't do it at tempo yet), that's chunk-phase work wearing a run costume - drop the speed, three clean reps, climb again. The method isn't failing; it's pointing.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Three dated recordings of consecutive full runs; a chart covered in ticks and `▷▷▷`; a log entry in caps with the blank filled; one human who has heard it; and Song One in the weekly maintenance rotation. That's a finished song. It's been a while - take the moment.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Mid-ritual, run two, you fully botch the solo entry. What does the count do, and what do your hands do?
2. Why does the ritual demand three *consecutive* intended runs rather than the best three of the evening?
3. Your run keeps disintegrating at verse 3 of a song whose verse you can play perfectly. Diagnose using module 1's vocabulary.
4. The finish is done and Song One's remaining flaws itch. Make the case, in one sentence each, for polishing versus starting Song Two - then say which one the course orders and why.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. The count survives if the run does: play through, rejoin at the next landmark, finish the take. Hands keep time; only stopping resets the count.
2. Consecutive-intended is performance conditions - no do-overs exists is the exact pressure the campfire and living-room gig will apply. Best-of-N is practice wearing a bow.
3. Not a finger problem - it's full-run stamina/attention, the skill fragments never train. The fix is more runs (and knowing verse 3 is where minds wander, so aiming attention there), not more verse chunking.
4. Polish: this song could be genuinely good. Song Two: the repertoire compounds by count, and standards rise free with volume. The course orders Song Two - clean-enough was defined in advance precisely to be obeyed now.

</details>
