# 3 · Songs 3-4 + leading the singing

> **You'll learn:** to finish the set's peak and closer, and the performance craft that turns four songs into a *led* sing-along - cueing, inviting, holding tempo against a dragging crowd, and landing the night.

## Why this matters

A complete set played *at* people is a recital; the campfire wants a *leader* - someone who invites voices in, holds the song steady underneath them, and makes forty strangers-to-music feel like a choir. Leading is a real skill with learnable moves, none of which require charisma you don't have: they're mostly timing, eye contact, and deciding things in advance - which by now you'll recognise as this course's entire philosophy wearing a woolly hat.

## The big picture

Finish the remaining two songs through the standard pipeline (nothing new there - the peak song, likely Stand By Me or Wonderwall, adds only its own wrinkles: Stand By Me's bass-line intro riff is worth having; Wonderwall's strum is the one place the anchor pattern earns a stretch). The new material is the leading layer, which sits *on top* of finished songs:

```text
THE LEADER'S JOBS
├── announce      intros people recognise, a word of welcome ("you all know this one")
├── invite        the cue face + the lifted chin at the chorus - permission to sing, granted visibly
├── hold          your tempo is the floor; crowds drag - the floor does not
├── rescue        crowd loses the verse words? you sing louder and clearer, they re-latch
└── land          the slowed last line, the ring, the grin - done together
```

## The moves, one by one

**The cue.** Crowds sing when explicitly invited, and the invitation is physical: at the chorus's approach, look *up from the guitar* (this is why automation mattered - eyes free are a leading tool), catch two or three faces, lift your chin on the last beat before the chorus, and sing the first word *bigger*. That's the whole move. It reads as effortless generosity; it's actually a rehearsed cue with a name, and choirs, worship leaders, and pub singers all run the identical mechanic.

**Holding against the drag.** Group singing drags - forty relaxed voices are a brake on tempo, always in the same direction. The leader's rule inverts the duo rule: *with the drummer you follow; with a crowd you hold.* Your strum is the band now, and politely immovable - the crowd re-syncs to you within a line (they're following, even when loud). Practice below simulates this; it's a strange sensation the first time, like walking against a gentle current.

**The verse retreat.** Crowds know choruses and mumble verses - design for it: verses are *yours* (sing them clearly at full commitment; this is where your module-4 work shines solo), choruses are *theirs* (you can even drop your voice to 70% once they're roaring - the quiet trick from lesson 2, applied to yourself). The set breathes: verse-intimacy, chorus-communion, four times an hour.

**Talking between songs.** Two sentences maximum, capo already moving while you speak ("this one's for the parents... you'll know it by bar two"). Patter's job is continuity of warmth, not comedy - and the capo-while-talking multitask is, yes, one more automation payoff.

> [!TIP]
> Build the **fire kit** now and it's a grab-and-go forever: the setlist card (laminated or phone-photographed), capo clipped to the headstock, a spare pick in the case pocket, phone tuner. Darkness plus firelight defeats music stands and memory alike - the kit assumes both failures in advance. Module 7 adds nothing to this kit but nerves.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why singing together does what it does (the science of the campfire)</summary>

Group singing is one of the most-studied happiness interventions that exists: synchronized vocalization measurably raises oxytocin and pain thresholds, lowers cortisol, and produces the fastest social-bonding effect researchers have found between strangers - faster than conversation, shared meals, or team games. Anthropologists' best guess is that it long predates language as social glue. Which reframes the leader's role: you're not performing *for* the circle, you're operating the machinery that bonds it - the guitar is a group-cohesion device and you're its trained operator. Practical corollary, sincerely: nobody around the fire is auditing your barre chords. They're getting an oxytocin bath with a soundtrack, and they will remember the *feeling* and the fact that *you made it happen*. This is the lowest-stakes, highest-gratitude venue in all of music - which is exactly why the course routes your first real performances through it.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - finish the set, rehearse the leading

1. **Pipeline songs 3 and 4** (peak and closer) with full arrangement shorthand. Stand By Me: learn the bass-riff intro (it's four notes and instantly recognised); Wonderwall, if chosen: its strum is a famous wrist-loosener - give it patient automation reps before laddering the vocal.
2. **Cue rehearsal, alone**: run the peak song, and at every chorus deliberately look up, catch the eyes of three objects placed around the room (mugs work; name them if it helps), chin-lift, first word bigger. Ridiculous, effective, five minutes per session for a week.
3. **The drag drill**: play the chorus loop while a *slowed-down* recording of the song plays quietly (90% speed) - hold your tempo against it for 8 bars. Uncomfortable = working. (A phone in a slipper makes an excellent dragging crowd.)
4. **Full set run-throughs, fire-kit conditions**: all four songs, setlist card only, standing, capo moves between songs, patter sentences spoken aloud to the empty room. Three runs across a week, recorded. The set is finished when a run needs nothing but the card.
5. **The soft launch**: family sing-along, lyric sheets distributed, all four songs, leading moves live. Log the debrief: which cue landed, where they dragged, which song they hijacked (one gets hijacked; it's a compliment). Setlist card: 4/4 FINISHED, dated.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if looking up costs you the guitar part</summary>

That's the talk test failing under a new load - eyes-up is spotlight-hungry until practiced. Retreat one rung: run the chorus with eyes closed (weirdly, easier than eyes-up - no visual processing at all), then eyes on one fixed point, then the three-mug sweep. It automates like everything else; the ladder is transferable, which was always its point.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. The crowd drags the chorus badly. State the leader's rule, why it differs from the duo rule, and what the crowd does next.
2. Anatomy of the cue: four physical components, in order.
3. Why are verses "yours" and choruses "theirs" - and what does the design buy the set's energy?
4. From the deep dive: what is actually being manufactured around the fire, and how does knowing that change pre-gig nerves?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Hold - you are the band, the floor doesn't move. The duo rule (follow the drummer) applies to a *rhythm section partner*; a singing crowd is a following ensemble, and it re-latches to a steady leader within a line. Confidence is the tempo's only defence.
2. Eyes up from the guitar → catch specific faces → chin lift on the last pre-chorus beat → first chorus word sung bigger. (Rehearsable on mugs.)
3. Crowds know choruses and mumble verses - so verses get your full clear voice (intimacy, and nobody left exposed) and choruses get their roar (communion, with you droppable to 70%). The alternation is the set breathing - four cycles of tension and release per song.
4. Group bonding - oxytocin, synchrony, the oldest social glue - with you as operator rather than exhibit. Nerves reframe accordingly: nobody is auditing; everybody is being given something, and they know who from.

</details>
