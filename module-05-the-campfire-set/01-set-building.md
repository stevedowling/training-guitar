# 1 · Set building - keys, capos, and a running order

> **You'll learn:** to design a sing-along set before learning it - checking every song against your voice, moving keys with a capo and the number system, and ordering songs so a campfire actually sings.

## Why this matters

A campfire set isn't four songs you like - it's four songs *people can sing with you*, in keys *your voice survives*, ordered so the energy builds instead of stalling. Set design is cheap (an afternoon with a capo and a notepad) and it prevents the expensive mistake: spending three weeks finishing a song only to discover the chorus lives a fourth above your range. Design first, then learn - the electric track taught the same lesson as "chart first, then chunk".

## The big picture

Every candidate song passes through three gates before it earns practice time:

```text
GATE 1: THE VOICE       sing the chorus a cappella - comfortable? strained? impossible?
        └── fix: move the key (capo up, or transpose down) until comfortable
GATE 2: THE HANDS       are the chords (in the chosen key/capo position) inside your anchor-pattern comfort?
        └── fix: capo position that yields easy shapes - that's half of what capos are FOR
GATE 3: THE CROWD       will people who don't play guitar sing this chorus uninvited?
        └── no fix - this one's a filter. Beloved > impressive, every time.
```

The candidates from the module stub all pre-clear gate 3 (they were chosen for it); gates 1 and 2 are personal and today's actual work.

## Finding your keys: the crude-but-sufficient method

No vocal coach needed for campfire purposes:

1. Sing the song's chorus along with the recording. If it's comfortable - done, learn it in the original key like the record.
2. If it strains high (the common case for untrained voices vs. professional singers): try singing it *an octave down* - sometimes that's instantly right.
3. Still awkward? The song needs a new key. Sing the chorus a cappella wherever your voice *wants* it, then find that first note on the guitar - the distance between the record's note and yours is how far to move.

Moving is what capos and numbers make trivial:

- **Same shapes, higher key**: capo up N frets, play identical shapes. (Song in G feels low? Capo 2, same G-shapes, now in A.)
- **Lower key, or easier shapes**: this is where module 2's number system pays out. Write the song as numbers once (`1 5 6m 4`), then spend them in any key: in G that's G-D-Em-C (the friendliest shapes in folk); in A it's A-E-F#m-D. Pick the key your *voice* chose, then the capo position that makes the *shapes* friendly - both gates, one decision.

> [!TIP]
> Build a one-line **key card** per song and staple it to the chart: `STAND BY ME · numbers: 1 6m 4 5 · my key: A · play: G-shapes capo 2 · first note: inside the A chord`. Five facts, and the song is singable tonight and transposable forever - including the campfire moment when someone else wants to sing it in *their* key, which is now a capo slide instead of a crisis.

## The running order

Sets have shapes, and the campfire shape is well-known:

| Slot | Job | Your likely pick |
|---|---|---|
| Opener | instantly recognisable, easy for *you* - settles your nerves and gathers the crowd | Three Little Birds (three chords, kids join by line two) |
| Second | the workhorse - everyone's warmed up, keep them | Knockin' on Heaven's Door (four chords, chorus everyone knows) |
| Third | the peak - biggest singalong chorus you own | Stand By Me, or Wonderwall if the crowd skews right |
| Closer | singable ember - gentler, melodic, lets the night land | Horse with No Name (already finished! the set's anchor was module 4's gift) |

Principles behind it: open with *your* easiest (nerves spend spotlight; module 4 taught you the spotlight is finite), peak on the *crowd's* biggest, close gentle, and never stack two similar-feel songs adjacent. Write the order on one card - the **setlist card** - because dark plus firelight plus memory is module 7's problem, and the card is its solution.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why these four progressions run the entire campfire genre</summary>

Run the numbers on the candidate list and a pattern jumps out: Three Little Birds is `1 4 5`, Knockin' is `1 5 6m` / `1 5 4`, Stand By Me is `1 6m 4 5` (the "50s progression" - literally hundreds of songs), Wonderwall's verse is a `6m 4 1 5` rotation of the same crayons. Four progressions - `1 4 5`, `1 5 6m 4` and its rotations, the 12-bar, and `1 6m 4 5` - cover a genuinely absurd share of the western songbook, which is why campfire veterans seem to "know every song": they know four number patterns and hear which one is playing. This is the compounding the module-2 deep dive promised: every key card you write makes the next song cheaper, and somewhere around card ten, songs stop being *learned* and start being *recognised*. When someone at the fire says "do you know [song you've never played]?", the honest campfire answer is often "hum it" - because it's going to be one of the four.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - design the whole set

An afternoon, a capo, a notepad, zero new songs learned:

1. **Gate-check all five candidates** (the stub's list): chorus sung with the recording, strain noted, octave-down tried. Verdict per song: original key / needs moving / gate-3 doubt (allowed! swap in your own beloved candidate if one fails you personally).
2. **Write the key card** for each survivor: numbers (from any chord sheet - translating letters to numbers is the exercise), your key, capo-and-shapes decision, first-note-from-chord.
3. **Choose the four** and the running order against the slot table - one sentence of reasoning per slot on the setlist card. (Horse likely closes; it's finished and gentle. If Wonderwall survived your gates, decide third-vs-closer honestly.)
4. **Test-drive the set on paper**: total time (~3-4 min/song = a 15-minute set - exactly right for a fire; longer sets are for module 7's ambitions), key variety (four songs in one key gets samey - a capo move between songs is also a natural moment to talk), and the energy curve read aloud.
5. File everything in the repo: `setlist-campfire.md` with the order card and four key cards. Lessons 2 and 3 learn the songs; tonight the set *exists*, which - as with Song One's chart - converts fog into a checklist.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the number translation stalls</summary>

Anchor on the key's 1-chord (the chord sheet's key, usually the first/last chord), then count letter distances up the major scale: in G, count G(1) A(2) B(3) C(4) D(5) E(6) F#(7) - so Em is 6m, C is 4, D is 5. Two songs in, it's automatic; that speed *is* the deep dive's promise starting to pay.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. A song strains your voice in its record key of C. Your comfortable chorus starts a third lower, in A. Give two ways to play it in A, and what decides between them.
2. Why does the opener slot go to *your* easiest song rather than the crowd's favourite?
3. From the deep dive: someone at the fire requests a song you've never played. Walk the veteran's move.
4. What are the three gates, and which one has no workaround?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Play A-shapes directly (A, D, E territory), or play G-shapes with capo 2 - decided by which shape-set is friendlier for the song's feel and your anchor pattern (and G-shapes with a capo win surprisingly often; folk lives there).
2. Nerves tax the spotlight hardest on song one - spending the opener on automated material buys settled hands and voice for the rest; the crowd's favourite lands better at the peak anyway.
3. "Hum it" - identify which of the four progressions it is by ear, find the 1-chord near their voice, and accompany from numbers. (Not guaranteed - but it works often enough to look like sorcery.)
4. Voice (fix: move the key), hands (fix: capo position for easy shapes), crowd (no fix - beloved beats impressive, and a song people won't sing isn't a campfire song regardless of its other virtues).

</details>
