# 2 · Songs 1-2: the two-and-three-chord heart

> **You'll learn:** to finish the set's opener and workhorse - two songs whose simplicity is the point - and the solo-acoustic arrangement moves that make one guitar sound like enough.

## Why this matters

The set's first two songs (likely Three Little Birds and Knockin' on Heaven's Door, per your lesson-1 cards) are harmonically tiny - three and four chords of anchor-pattern strumming. That's not a beginner compromise; it's the genre's actual architecture, and it frees this lesson's real subject: **arrangement** - the craft of making one acoustic guitar and one voice carry a whole song. Simple songs are where arrangement is learnable, because nothing else is competing for the spotlight.

## The big picture

The full pipeline for each song is now entirely made of tools you own:

```text
key card (done) → chart with breath marks → automate the guitar full-song (talk test)
→ ladder the vocal per section → chain sung joins → runs, never stop → finish ritual → weekly set
```

What this lesson adds is the arrangement layer - the decisions that go *on* the chart before the automation reps begin, because they change what you're automating:

## The solo-acoustic arrangement kit

**Dynamics as structure** (the accompanist's art, now formalized): with no band, *you* are the arrangement, and the cheapest big move is a dynamic plan per section. The standard shape - verses at 60% (lighter wrist, maybe fewer strings), choruses at 100% (full strums, open wrist), and one section somewhere at 40% (a breakdown verse or final chorus first-half, quiet enough that voices around the fire suddenly hear themselves - which reliably makes them sing *louder*; it's the oldest sing-along trick there is).

**The strum vocabulary, minimally expanded**: the anchor pattern (D-DU-D-DU) remains home base; add two variations only - the *push* (tie across beat 3: D-DU-_U-DU) for choruses that want lean, and *stabs* (single downstrums on beat 1, letting it ring) for the quiet sections. Three patterns total. Resist more: pattern-switching taxes the spotlight that the voice is renting.

**Intros people recognise**: a campfire intro's job is announcement - people should know the song inside four bars. The reliable recipe: play the chorus's chord loop once with the anchor pattern, then sing. (Iconic riff intros - Knockin's gentle descent, say - are worth having *if* they're automated; a flubbed fancy intro spends confidence the opener slot exists to build.)

**Endings for singers**: campfire endings are shared property - the crowd is singing too and needs the landing signalled. The universal one: final chorus, last line *slowed noticeably* (rallentando - you control it; you're the band), one big let-ring chord. The slowdown is audible to everyone as "here's the end"; nobody is left singing into silence.

> [!TIP]
> Write the arrangement on the chart in your own shorthand - `V1 60% anchor · CH 100% push · V3 40% stabs · END rall+ring` - and then *practice the plan, not just the song*. An arrangement that lives only in intentions evaporates at the fire; one that got fifty automated reps is simply how the song goes now.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why simple songs are harder to play well (the exposure problem)</summary>

Experienced players routinely butcher three-chord songs after nailing complex ones, and the reason is exposure: dense arrangements hide micro-flaws (a rushed change vanishes inside a busy riff), while an anchor-pattern song is acoustic glass - every uneven strum, every hesitated change, every dynamic non-decision is fully audible, because nothing else is happening. This is why session legends speak of simple rhythm parts with reverence, and why this lesson's two songs deserve real reps despite reading as "easy" on the key card. The upside of glass: it's also where *good* fundamentals become fully audible - a steady anchor pattern with a real dynamic plan sounds astonishingly professional on its own, which is the entire secret of every campfire hero you've admired. They're not doing more than you can; they're doing less, evenly, on purpose.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - finish the opener and the workhorse

Run both songs through the pipeline; they'll overlap comfortably across two-ish weeks of daytime slots:

1. **Arrange first**: chart both songs with full arrangement shorthand - dynamic plan, pattern per section, intro recipe, singers' ending, breath marks. (Fifteen minutes each, and the fifty-rep clock starts *after* this, not before.)
2. **Automate and ladder** per the pipeline. Three Little Birds should move startlingly fast (three chords, repetitive lyric - the ladder collapses rungs); Knockin' adds the 6m chord and a slower feel worth savouring. Log rung numbers as ever.
3. **The exposure drill**, once per song (deep dive made the case): record 8 bars of naked anchor pattern at the song's tempo, listen at half speed for evenness - the electric-track recording test, acoustic edition. Uneven? Five minutes of loud/quiet-game reps; re-record.
4. **Finish ritual both songs** - with the living-room audience now explicitly *invited to sing* (put the lyric sheet in front of them; opener songs deserve opener rehearsals). Their singing over your playing is the module's first true field test: if your voice holds its melody while theirs wander - and wander they will - the automation is real.
5. Update `setlist-campfire.md`: two of four slots marked FINISHED, with dates. Half a set exists.

One or two exposures is genuinely all it takes; the skill was pre-built in module 3 (or, if you took the campfire track first, consider this the preview of why module 3 exists).

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. What are the three strum patterns in the minimal vocabulary, and what does each one's *job* description say?
2. Why does the quiet section make a campfire sing louder - and where does the trick come from in this course's own toolkit?
3. From the deep dive: why can a player who nails Song Two's 176 bpm chug still sound bad on a three-chord ballad?
4. Design an ending for Three Little Birds using the singers' recipe - then say what the crowd hears that tells them it's landing.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Anchor (D-DU-D-DU): home base, verses and default. Push (tied 3): chorus lean and lift. Stabs (ringing 1s): the quiet sections and breakdowns. Three jobs, three patterns, no more.
2. Dropping your volume lets the crowd suddenly hear themselves, and self-hearing recruits confidence and volume - it's the Loud/Quiet dynamics game from the duo sessions, played on an audience instead of a drummer.
3. Exposure: the ballad is acoustic glass - unevenness and non-decisions that a dense arrangement hides are fully audible when only six strings and a voice exist. Evenness under simplicity is its own trained skill.
4. Final chorus, last line slowed markedly ("every little thing... is gonna be all... right..."), one big let-ring chord. The audible slowdown *is* the signal - everyone lands the last word together and nobody sings into the silence.

</details>
