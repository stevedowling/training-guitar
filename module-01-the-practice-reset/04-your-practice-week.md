# 4 · Your practice week - a system that survives real life

> **You'll learn:** to design a practice week around your actual life - night headphone sessions, occasional loud daytime slots, a drummer with a bedtime - and a log that keeps the system honest.

## Why this matters

The method from lesson 3 works *per session*; this lesson makes sessions *happen*. Practice systems fail at the calendar, not the fretboard - the perfect routine that needs a free quiet hour daily is a fantasy, while a modest one that fits Tuesday-as-it-actually-is compounds forever. You have specific real conditions: wireless headphones for silent night practice, some daytime freedom, a young drummer with her own (reluctant) practice life. Design for those, not for an imaginary schedule.

## The big picture

The unit of progress is a **20-minute session with a shape**, not a marathon:

```text
THE STANDARD SESSION (~20 min, headphones, any night)
├── 2 min   warm-up: one repertoire song, full run        ← maintenance, disguised
├── 13 min  THE WORK: current song, current phase
│           (chunk a section / chain a join - chart says which)
├── 4 min   one full run, never stop, phone recording
└── 1 min   log it: one line. next session's first move.
```

Three of those a week is a finished song roughly every 2-4 weeks - a **12-18 song repertoire within a year**, from twenty-minute nights. The fragment years weren't short of hours; they were short of *shape*.

## Matching session types to your real week

Different slots afford different work - planning this in advance beats deciding at 9pm with a guitar on your knee:

| Slot | What it's for | What it's bad at |
|---|---|---|
| **Night + headphones** (most sessions) | chunk/chain electric work, full runs with the recording in your ears - honestly most of the course | singing (you'll wake the house), acoustic (it's loud, no headphone jack) |
| **Daytime loud** (1-2× week, precious) | acoustic + campfire track, *singing while playing* (module 4's work lives here), amp-up electric for feel | wasting on things headphones handle - guard these slots for noise-requiring work |
| **Duo time with the drummer** (short, opportunistic) | module 3/6 material - count-ins, simple grooves, jam games. 10 minutes counts. | anything resembling a lesson for her - it's a jam, or it's nothing (module 6 is entirely about this) |

That's the whole scheduling insight: **assign each track's work to the slot that affords it**. Electric/band track lives at night; singing/campfire track lives in daytime slots; duo material piggybacks on *her* practice moments (a drummer who gets to play an actual song with an actual band - you - practices more; this is the course's happiest side effect).

## The log: one line, no ceremony

In the repo or a notebook - `practice-log.md`, newest at top:

```markdown
## Song One: <title>          chart: charts/song-one.md
- 2026-07-14 · chained V→C join, 2+2 clean at tempo · run ▷▷ · NEXT: chorus bars 5-8 slow
- 2026-07-12 · chunked chorus ×3 @ 80% · run ▷ ugly at solo join · NEXT: V→C join
```

The line that matters is **NEXT** - it's tomorrow's 9pm decision, made tonight while the problem is fresh. Sessions that start with a decision already made skip the noodling-while-deciding trap entirely, which is where 20-minute sessions go to die.

> [!TIP]
> Add a weekly one-liner: `WEEK: runs 5 · joins cleared 2 · repertoire played: SNA ✓`. Ten seconds, and after a month the file becomes the motivational document - visible, compounding proof that the system finishes things. Your audit file from lesson 1 is page one of that story on purpose.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: the 80/20 noodling clause, and why streaks beat sessions</summary>

Two sustainability clauses, because rigid systems get abandoned by February. First: **noodling is legal** - 20% of any session (or whole bonus sessions) can be pure play, new-riff flirtation, feedback worship, whatever. The fragment loop was never wrong to *enjoy*; it was wrong to be the *whole diet*. A system that bans joy loses to joy eventually; a system that budgets joy keeps both. The one rule: the 13 work-minutes come first - noodling is dessert, not dinner.

Second: when life wrecks a week, protect the *streak*, not the duration - a 5-minute session (one full run of the current song, logged) keeps the chain alive and takes less time than the guilt of skipping. Habit research is boringly consistent here: frequency builds identity, duration builds skill, and identity ("I'm someone who practices") is what survives busy months. Five minutes is never pointless; it's the system refusing to die.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - design the week, run the first session

Paper planning plus one real session:

1. **Map your real week**: which 3-4 evenings reliably offer 20 headphone-minutes? Which 1-2 daytime windows offer noise freedom? When does drum practice (attemptedly) happen? Write an actual timetable - `practice-plan.md` - with a session *type* in each slot. Be pessimistic; a plan you beat feels better than one that shames you.
2. **Stock each slot's first move**: from Song One's chart, write the NEXT line for your first three sessions (e.g., "Tue night: chunk chorus · Thu night: chain V→C · Sat day: acoustic - just play, module 4 will claim this slot soon").
3. **Set up the night rig properly** when the headphones arrive: template the volume/tone once, note the settings in the plan file. A rig that's 30 seconds from playable makes 9:45pm sessions happen; one that needs 5 minutes of setup quietly kills them. (This tiny friction fact ruins more practice systems than any musical difficulty.)
4. **Run one standard session tonight** - the full 20-minute shape, ending with the log line and a NEXT. This is the course's actual first rep.
5. Optional but characteristic: tell the drummer the plan exists, and that some sessions have her name on them. Nine-year-olds hold you to things magnificently - it's free accountability.

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

A `practice-plan.md` with a realistic weekly shape and typed slots; three pre-written NEXT lines; one honest logged session with a run recorded; and (soon) a night rig with its settings noted. Module 1 complete - the system exists. The rest of the course is songs to run through it: [Module 2](../module-02-first-full-songs/) starts the electric/band track, [Module 4](../module-04-singing-while-playing/) starts the campfire track, and they're happily parallel.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. It's 9:40pm Tuesday. Under the old system, describe the first five minutes of practice. Under this system, what are they - and what made the difference?
2. Saturday afternoon, house empty, amp allowed. Which work should this slot get and which would *waste* it - specifically for your two tracks?
3. Brutal week: it's Thursday, zero sessions so far, you have 6 minutes. What does the system prescribe, and why is it genuinely better than nothing?
4. Where does noodling live in the new system - banned, unlimited, or something else?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Old: deciding what to play by playing things - i.e., noodling into the fragment loop. New: the log's NEXT line is the decision, made last session - warm-up run, then straight to the named join or section. The difference is *when the decision was made*.
2. It gets the noise-requiring track: singing-while-playing work and acoustic campfire songs (plus amp-loud electric for feel if time remains). Wasted on: silent-capable chunking that Tuesday's headphones handle fine.
3. One full run of the current song, logged, streak intact - five minutes. Better than nothing because the streak *is* the system's survival, and a full run is never empty calories (it's a recovery rep and repertoire maintenance in one).
4. Budgeted: 20% dessert after the 13 work-minutes, plus guilt-free bonus sessions. Joy is load-bearing; unbudgeted joy just runs the old loop.

</details>
