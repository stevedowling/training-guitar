# 3 · The finishing method - chunk, chain, perform

> **You'll learn:** the three-phase method that turns a charted song into a finished one - including the never-stop rule that's the opposite of how you currently practice.

## Why this matters

You now have a chart and a Song One. The remaining question is *what to actually do in a session* - because "practice the song" is exactly the vague instruction that dissolves into noodling. The method here is how musicians who finish things structure the work: it's phase-based, each phase has a different rule, and the phase most players skip is the one that makes performance possible.

## The big picture

```text
PHASE 1: CHUNK        PHASE 2: CHAIN           PHASE 3: PERFORM
learn each section    weld sections together   full runs, never stop
─────────────────     ────────────────────     ─────────────────────
one section at a      pairs of sections,       start to finish with the
time, slowly, until   focusing the JOINS       recording - mistakes get
smooth ×3             your chart starred       played THROUGH, not fixed
"can I play it?"      "can I get between       "can I keep going?"
                       them?"
```

A song moves left to right, but not all at once - the chorus can be chaining while the solo still chunks. The chart tracks it: tick sections as they clear each phase. The phases have opposite rules about mistakes, and knowing which rule applies *right now* is the method.

## Phase 1 - Chunk: slow is the tool

Learning a section, the rule is the one you've heard forever and it remains true: **slower than you want, sped up only when clean**. Three clean repetitions at one speed buys a small tempo bump; a flub sends the metronome back down. The pace feels insulting; the alternative - practicing the mistake at full speed until it's permanent - is how fragments got rough in the first place.

You're good at this phase (it's what riff-learning is). One upgrade: chunk *with the bar counts from your chart*, so "the verse" means all 16 bars including the boring bar 14 - fragments have a habit of being the interesting 80% of a section.

## Phase 2 - Chain: the joins are the song

Nobody's full run collapses in the middle of the chorus - it collapses at bar 1 of the chorus, on the join. Joins fail because they were never practiced: every fragment starts *at* a section boundary and ends before the next, so years of practice contain zero reps of the two beats that connect them. Your chart's stars mark the debt.

Chaining pays it directly: take the last 2 bars of section A plus the first 2 of section B, and loop *that four-bar unit* like it's a riff. Then last-4 + first-4. Then A→B whole. Every starred join gets this treatment - it's mechanical, fast (joins are tiny), and the single highest-value practice this course will ask of you, because it's the exact skill your years of playing skipped.

## Phase 3 - Perform: the never-stop rule

The phase that makes the difference, with the rule that feels wrong:

> **In a full run, mistakes are played *through*, never fixed. Flub a chord - keep strumming. Lose bar 3 of the solo - rejoin at bar 5. The run only fails if you stop.**

Why it feels wrong: every instinct says stop-and-fix, because in Phase 1 that *was* the rule. Why it's right: performance is a different skill from accuracy, and its core move is *continuing* - the audience (campfire, daughter on drums) barely registers a flubbed note but absolutely registers a stop. Recovery - finding the way back in without the train derailing - is learnable, improves fast, and can only be practiced by not stopping. Full runs are recovery reps wearing a song costume.

Run with the recording (it's a merciless, perfect metronome that also happens to be the arrangement) - and count runs on the chart: `▷▷▷` filling in. **Three clean-enough runs, recorded on the phone: the song is finished.** Then - this matters - it enters the *repertoire*: one full play-through a week keeps it alive while the next song starts. Finished songs are maintained, not abandoned; that's the difference between a setlist and a longer fragment pile.

> [!TIP]
> "Clean-enough" is deliberate. Three *flawless* runs is a perfectionist's trap that reopens the fragment door ("not ready, better keep polishing" is noodling in a suit). Clean-enough = a listener who doesn't play guitar would call it "playing the song". The capstone will sharpen your standards; the first finishes need the bar where it is.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why slow practice and never-stop don't contradict</summary>

They target different memory systems. Chunking builds *motor accuracy* - myelinating the precise finger sequence - and error-free slow reps are how that circuitry lays down clean (practicing mistakes literally wires the mistakes). Performing builds *retrieval under load* - executing while attention is taxed by tempo, memory, nerves, a drummer - and that system strengthens through exactly the ugly, effortful, error-adjacent reps the first system forbids. Musicians' shorthand: *practice slow to build it, perform through to own it*. The classic amateur error is applying one rule everywhere: all-slow-and-fix produces studio hands that collapse live (stop-start habit under load), all-run-throughs produces sloppy hands that never tighten. The phases exist so each rule operates where it works - and knowing which phase you're in at any moment is, honestly, most of what a good teacher provides.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - one section through all three phases

Tonight's session, headphones on, ~25 minutes, Song One's chart on the stand:

1. **Pick the highest-value target**: the *easiest unlearned section* (fastest win) - or if most sections are ticked already, go straight to the joins. Chunk it: slow, chart-complete, three clean reps before each speed-up. (~10 min)
2. **Chain one starred join** with the 2+2 → 4+4 → whole method - ideally the join *into* tonight's section. (~5 min)
3. **One full run, never stop** - yes, already, tonight, with sections you haven't learned: strum muted strings in rhythm through the unknown parts, rejoin where you know it. This feels ridiculous and does two important things: proves the never-stop rule is survivable, and shows you the whole song's shape from inside. With the recording, phone recording you. (~4 min)
4. Listen back to 30 seconds of the recording (any 30). Write one sentence on the chart: what actually broke - fingers, memory, or joins? (It's nearly always joins. Now you know what tomorrow's session is.)

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the full run feels unbearable</summary>

That's the stop-and-fix habit objecting, not a real problem. Two crutches, both legitimate: drop the recording to 75% speed (YouTube and most apps do this), and/or let "playing through" mean *one strum on beat 1 of each bar* through hard parts. The run's job tonight is continuity, not content. Content is what the next two weeks of chunking are for.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

A chart with one new section tick, one join showing chain-work, a `▷` for run one, and a one-sentence diagnosis. Plus a phone recording you cringed at - keep it. It's the "before" photo, and module 2's finish will want the comparison.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. You're mid-full-run and completely botch the bridge entry. Phase 3 rule: what do you do in the next two seconds, and what would doing the "responsible" thing (stop, fix it) actually practice?
2. Which phase does each session activity belong to, and is it being done right: (a) looping the chorus's last 2 bars + solo's first 2, (b) playing the verse at full speed and stopping at every flub, (c) playing the intro you've known for years, again?
3. Why does the method demand the *recording* for Phase 3 runs rather than your own tempo?
4. From the deep dive: what does all-slow-and-fix practice produce, and what does all-run-through practice produce?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Keep time (strum, mute, anything) and rejoin at the next landmark - probably bridge bar 3 or the next section. Stopping would practice stopping - the exact reflex that ends campfire songs and confuses drummers.
2. (a) Phase 2, textbook. (b) Phase 1 rule at Phase 3 speed - wrong on both ends: too fast to fix cleanly, too stop-start to build runs. Slow it down *or* commit to the run. (c) No phase - that's the fluency illusion having a snack. One run of known material as warm-up is fine; the third repeat is hiding.
3. The recording never slows for your hard bit, never waits while you reset - it's performance conditions on demand, plus the arrangement details your memory smooths over. (And it's the duet partner that trains you for the human one in module 3.)
4. Studio hands that collapse live, and sloppy hands that never tighten - each rule missing its other half.

</details>
