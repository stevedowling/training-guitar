# 2 · Song anatomy - seeing the whole song at once

> **You'll learn:** to map any song onto one page - sections, bar counts, and where the tricky joins live - so "learn the song" becomes a checklist instead of a fog.

## Why this matters

One reason fragments win: a riff is *visible* (eight bars, you can see the end) while "the whole song" is fog - an unknown quantity of stuff somewhere behind the intro. Mapping dissolves the fog. A three-and-a-half-minute rock song is typically just 4-6 distinct parts arranged in a pattern, and once you've charted that pattern, you know exactly what's left to learn and exactly how close the finish line is. Every song in this course starts with its map.

## The big picture

Here's a one-page map of a song you already half-know - *Seven Nation Army* (The White Stripes). Chords and structure are facts about the song; listen to the recording with this in front of you:

```text
SEVEN NATION ARMY - The White Stripes         ~124 bpm, 4/4
Distinct parts: THE RIFF (E-based, 7 notes) · VERSE (riff continues) · CHORUS ("...army" - E5/G5/A5 power stabs) · SOLO (riff, octave up)

INTRO    | riff ×4          |  8 bars   ✅ can play
VERSE 1  | riff under vocal | 16 bars   ✅ same riff!
CHORUS 1 | power stabs      |  8 bars   ⬜ learn
VERSE 2  | riff             | 16 bars   ✅ free
CHORUS 2 | stabs            |  8 bars   ⬜ same as chorus 1
SOLO     | riff, octave up  |  8 bars   ⬜ learn (it's the riff!)
VERSE 3  | riff             | 16 bars   ✅ free
CHORUS 3 | stabs ×2, ending | 12 bars   ⬜ chorus + 4-bar ending
```

Read what the map just revealed: this "whole song" is **one riff you already know, one 8-bar chorus, and an octave shift**. The fog was hiding maybe fifteen minutes of new material. This is the normal discovery - songs are far more repetitive than they feel from inside the fog, and the map turns that repetition into free progress.

## The vocabulary: section names

Rock songs assemble from a small standard kit - knowing the names lets you hear structure in real time:

| Section | Job | How to spot it |
|---|---|---|
| Intro | sets the groove, often the hook | before the singing |
| Verse | tells the story, changes lyrics each time | lower energy, repeated melody, new words |
| Chorus | the payoff, same words every time | loudest, the title usually lives here |
| Pre-chorus | ramp between the two | tension building, often 2-4 bars |
| Bridge | the "somewhere else" bit | appears once, ~2/3 through, different chords |
| Solo | you know this one | often over verse or chorus chords - free progress again |
| Outro | the landing | last chorus variation, riff fade, or hard stop |

Most rock songs are some shuffle of `Intro V C V C Bridge/Solo C C Out` - and blues adds the other great pattern, the **12-bar cycle**, where one 12-bar chord loop repeats for the whole song (module 6 will love this: it's the easiest full-song form for a young drummer too).

## Making a map: the 15-minute method

1. **Listen once doing nothing.** Whole song, no guitar, no pausing. (Harder than it sounds - the urge to grab the guitar at the first cool bit is the fragment loop knocking. It can wait three minutes.)
2. **Listen again with a pencil.** Every time the song changes rooms, write a section name. Don't count bars yet, just catch the shape: `I V C V C S C C`.
3. **Third listen: count bars.** Count "1-2-3-4" per bar, jot the totals. Rock sections are nearly always 4, 8, 12, or 16 bars - if you counted 7 or 9, recount (occasionally a song really does have an odd bar; finding one is a badge of honour, not an error).
4. **Mark what repeats.** Identical sections get the same mark - this is where the "80% of this song is three parts" discovery happens.
5. **Tick what you can already play**, and star the *joins* - the two beats where one section becomes the next. Joins are where full-song runs actually break down, and no fragment ever practiced them. They get their own treatment next lesson.

> [!TIP]
> Chords and structure are facts and freely shareable - this course gives you those. For full note-for-note detail, pair each map with the recording plus a licensed tab source (Songsterr, Ultimate Guitar's official tabs). The map is yours though - making it *is* the learning; a downloaded map teaches nothing.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why pros chart everything - and the number trick worth stealing</summary>

Session players walk into studios and record songs they heard for the first time that morning - entirely on one-page charts. Nashville players go further with the **number system**: writing chords as scale degrees (in G: G=1, C=4, D=5, so a verse is `1 4 5 4`) - which makes every chart key-proof. Why steal this now? Two reasons. Campfire reality: someone will ask for the song in a different key ("too high to sing!") - a capo and a number chart make that a non-event, and module 5 cashes this in. And pattern recognition: after charting five songs you'll notice the same numbers everywhere (1-4-5, 1-5-6-4, 12-bar's 1-1-1-1-4-4-1-1-5-4-1-1). Rock is a small number of skeletons wearing different clothes - each chart you make teaches the skeletons, and future songs get *faster to learn*. Charting compounds.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - chart two songs

Pencil, paper (or `charts/` in the repo), and the recordings:

1. **Chart Seven Nation Army yourself** - yes, the map above exists; make yours *without* it, then compare. Differences are instructive (bar counts drift where the solo sneaks in). 15 minutes.
2. **Chart Song One** (your pick from lesson 1) with the full method: shape, bar counts, repeat marks, ✅ ticks for what you already play, ⭐ stars on every join. This chart is module 2's (or your track's) working document - it rides on your music stand until the song is finished.
3. Read your Song One chart and answer in writing: how many *distinct* parts does it really have? What percentage can you already play? How many joins need work? (The answers are usually: fewer than felt, more than expected, and 4-6 joins - the fog rarely survives contact with a pencil.)
4. Bonus for the drum track ahead: note Song One's tempo (tap along with a metronome app until it matches) and write it on the chart. Module 3 wants every chart tempo-stamped.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if bar counting keeps slipping</summary>

Count out loud, and count the *drums*, not the vocal (singers float over the beat; drummers are the grid - a lesson your daughter will enjoy being told she's the authority on). If you lose the count, don't restart the song - catch the next section boundary and count from there. Section starts almost always land on a drum accent.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Two one-page charts. Seven Nation Army's should broadly match the lesson's (small differences fine). Song One's has shape, counts, tempo, ticks, and stars - and step 3's written answers, which are the real deliverable: the fog measured, and found smaller than advertised.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. A song's sections go `I V C V C B C C`. Which section appears once, what's it called, and roughly when will it arrive in a 3:30 song?
2. Your chart shows Verse 1, 2, and 3 are musically identical. You can play Verse 1. What's the *learning* cost of verses 2 and 3, and what's the *performing* cost nobody practices?
3. Why chart with pencil-and-ears when the internet has the tab already?
4. From the deep dive: in the key of A, what chords are `1 4 5` - and why would a campfire care?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. The bridge, once, around 2:20 (about two-thirds through) - knowing it's coming is half of playing it.
2. Learning: zero, it's free progress. Performing: the *stamina and attention* of playing the same part the third time without drifting - plus the joins into and out of it. That's full-run practice, and only full-run practice.
3. Because the chart's value is the listening it forces - structure heard is structure learned. The tab is the note detail; the map is the architecture, and architecture is what fragments never taught you.
4. A, D, E - and the campfire cares because when the singer needs it lower, `1 4 5` moves to G, C, D with a shrug, while letter-only knowledge starts over.

</details>
