# 1 · Why you know 100 riffs and 0 songs

> **You'll learn:** exactly why years of practice produced fragments instead of finished songs - and the one rule that reverses it.

## Why this matters

You can play. Years of it, hands that know their way around the neck, a head full of intros and riffs. And yet if someone handed you a guitar at a party tonight and said "play us something", the honest answer is a shuffle through fragments that trail off at the second verse. That gap isn't a talent problem or a discipline problem - it's a *system* problem, and systems can be swapped.

## The big picture

Here's the practice loop that produced the current situation - see if it reads familiar:

| The fragment loop | What's really happening |
|---|---|
| Pick up guitar, noodle warm-up | fun, zero direction |
| Play the riffs you already know | rehearsing the *known* - feels good, adds nothing |
| Try the cool intro from that song | novelty hit 🎉 - the best feeling in practice |
| Intro gets hard at the verse... | ...the novelty is gone and the *work* begins |
| Switch to a different cool intro | novelty hit 🎉 again |
| Put guitar down feeling like you practiced | you did - the first 20% of six different songs |

The trap is that **starting a song feels amazing and finishing one feels like work** - so an unstructured session always drifts toward starting. Nothing about playing for more years fixes this; it just grows the fragment collection.

The reversal is one rule:

> **One song at a time, finished before the next one starts. "Finished" means: played start to finish, with the recording, three times, recorded on your phone.**

Everything else in this course - song anatomy, the finishing method, the practice week - exists to make that rule survivable and even fun. But the rule is the course.

## What "finished" buys you that fragments can't

- **Compounding repertoire.** Fragments evaporate (when did you last play that riff you learned in 2019?). Finished songs get *performed* - for family, at the campfire, with your daughter - and performance is the deepest memorization there is. A repertoire maintains itself; a fragment pile doesn't.
- **The full-song skills.** Transitions between sections, stamina, recovering from mistakes mid-stream, dynamics across three minutes - none of these exist in bar one to eight. You currently have years of experience at *starting* songs and almost none at *playing* them. That's fixable fast, precisely because your hands are already good.
- **The finish line for others.** "Play us something" has an answer. Your daughter's drum practice has a *point* (module 6 leans hard on this). The campfire has a leader.

## Choosing Song One

Module 2 provides curated candidates, but the choosing criteria matter more than the choice - they'll pick every song you ever learn:

1. **You love it** - you'll hear it 200 times; like it or abandon it.
2. **It's at or below your hands** - the first *finished* song should spend your skill on finishing, not on new technique. Your fragment pile proves the hands are ahead of the repertoire; pick accordingly, and swallow the pride. A finished easy song beats an abandoned impressive one by exactly one song.
3. **Someone will hear it** - a song with a destination (the duo with your daughter, the campfire) gets finished; a song for the void gets fragmented.

> [!TIP]
> The pride thing deserves one more sentence, because it's the real obstacle for an experienced player: choosing an "easy" song feels like admitting something. Reframe it: session musicians - the best hired hands in the world - are defined by *finishing* simple parts flawlessly, not by fragments of hard ones. Finishing IS the advanced skill. It's the one you haven't practiced.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why your brain prefers fragments (it's not weakness)</summary>

Two well-studied effects conspire. First, novelty is genuinely rewarding: dopamine responds to *new* patterns more than repeated ones, so bar 1 of a new song out-feels bar 40 of a known one - every time, forever. Practice systems exist precisely because motivation reliably points the wrong way; pros don't have better willpower, they have structures that don't consult it. Second, the *fluency illusion*: re-playing the parts you know feels like skill (it's smooth! it sounds good!) while grinding a transition feels like failure (it's slow and ugly) - but learning research is unambiguous that the ugly, effortful rep is where change happens, and the smooth rep is close to neutral. Your practice hours were real; they were just spent at the two least productive spots on the curve - polished known material and abandoned novelty. The system in this course simply moves the same hours to the middle.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - the audit and the pick

No new playing today - today is honest inventory. Create a file (or a page in a notebook, but the repo works nicely - `practice-log.md` starts here):

1. **List your fragments.** Every riff, intro, and partial song you can play from memory *right now*. Be thorough - this list is usually 30+ items long and the length is the point. Next to each: which song it's from, and roughly what percentage of that song you can play.
2. **Count the finishes.** How many entries are at 100% - genuinely start to finish? (Any number including zero is fine; it's a baseline, not a verdict.)
3. **Circle the top 5** you'd most love to hear yourself play *complete*. Ignore difficulty for the circling; this is the desire list.
4. **Run the three criteria** over the circled five: love it / at-or-below the hands / has an audience. Write Song One at the top of the file. (If none survive criterion 2, module 2's candidates exist for exactly this reason - pick provisionally and stay open.)
5. Write yesterday's date next to a sentence: *"Last time I played a complete song for another human: ______."* Fill it honestly. The capstone's job is making that sentence embarrassing to remember.

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

A file with 20-50 fragments (each with song + %), a finish count (probably 0-2 - that's the normal starting state for exactly your kind of player), five circles, one Song One, and one uncomfortable sentence. Ten minutes, no guitar required, and you now know precisely what the next six modules are for.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. It's Tuesday night, headphones on, 20 minutes free. Old-you plays the intro to something new and cool. What does the fragment loop predict happens by Thursday - and what single question should new-you ask before playing a note?
2. Why does the definition of "finished" require *recording* three clean runs rather than just feeling like you can play it?
3. Your Song One candidate has one section clearly beyond your current hands. Keep it or swap it - and which criterion decides?
4. From the deep dive: why does re-playing polished material *feel* more productive than grinding a rough transition, and which one actually is?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. The loop predicts: novelty fades at the hard part, a new shinier intro appears, Thursday finds another fragment on the pile. The question: *"is this Song One - and if not, what does Song One need tonight?"*
2. Feelings are the fluency illusion's home turf - a recording is unfakeable evidence, catches the flubs your attention skips, and doubles as the memorization deep-cut that performance provides.
3. Swap it (or consciously park it for later) - criterion 2. The first finish must spend its budget on *finishing*; new technique is a different budget. It'll still be there as Song Four.
4. Smooth playing feels skillful and rough playing feels like failure - but change happens almost entirely in the effortful middle. The feeling and the learning are nearly opposite signals.

</details>
