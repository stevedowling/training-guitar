# 1 · The grid - timing practice that isn't boring

> **You'll learn:** to hear exactly where your playing sits against the beat, and to tighten it with drum loops and click games - because "pretty good timing" alone in a room becomes audible drift next to a drummer.

## Why this matters

Playing with another human is the whole point of the electric track - and the skill that makes duos work isn't speed or vocabulary, it's *placement*: notes landing where the beat is, not near it. Solo players never find out where they actually sit (there's nothing to disagree with); a drummer is a second opinion arriving four times per bar. This lesson gets the disagreement over with privately, against machines, before module 6 makes it a family matter.

## The big picture

Timing work has a bad reputation because it's taught as punishment (metronome, 40 bpm, misery). The reframe that fixes it:

> **The click is not a judge - it's a duet partner with perfect time and no opinions.** Every exercise in this lesson is "play with the band", where the band happens to be very small and very punctual.

And one honest fact to lower the stakes: nobody plays *on* the grid - good players place notes consistently in a tiny zone around it (ahead = eager, behind = relaxed; both are styles). The enemy is *wander* - eager in bar one, sleepy in bar nine - because wander is what fights a drummer.

## Hearing yourself honestly: the recording test

Before fixing anything, measure. Record 8 bars of Song Two's main riff against a drum loop at song tempo, then listen back at half speed (your phone or any audio app can slow playback):

| What you hear | What it means |
|---|---|
| guitar consistently *with* or slightly around the snare | you're locked - congratulations, this module goes fast |
| guitar early, especially at section starts | eagerness - the most common rock player lean; you're anticipating instead of listening |
| guitar late after fills or hard bits | attention debt - the hands borrow time from the beat when busy |
| all of the above in one take | wander - normal starting state, and exactly what the games below fix |

This test repeats at the end of the module; keep the recording.

## The games

**Drum loop over click, always.** A drum loop at song tempo (any free app; pick a rock beat) gives the same grid with groove attached - your ear locks to a snare far more naturally than to a beep. The click comes back below for one specific job.

**Game 1 - Landing lights (feel the "2 and 4").** Rock timing lives on the snare (beats 2 and 4). Play the riff accenting *with* the snare deliberately for 4 bars, then normally for 4. The accent bars calibrate; the normal bars test.

**Game 2 - The gap click.** Set the loop (or click) to drop out for a bar and return (many apps do this; or mute the speaker rhythmically with your palm... or recruit the drummer's practice pad app). Play through the silent bar and notice where the beat *actually* was when it returns. This is the stop-start skill from Should I Stay, generalized - internal time bridging external silence, and it's the single best predictor of duo readiness.

**Game 3 - Half-time ears.** Set the loop at *half* the song tempo and feel each click as beat "1 and 3" (or just 1). Fewer landmarks = more internal responsibility = the same song suddenly harder and more honest. When Song One survives half-time loops, its timing is real.

**Game 4 - Rush hunt.** Full run of any finished song with the loop, recording. Listen for the two classic rush points: section entries (excitement) and *after mistakes* (escape instinct). Naming your personal rush points is half of fixing them - write them on the chart like the joins were.

> [!TIP]
> Ten minutes of games inside normal sessions beats a dedicated timing night - timing is a spice, not a meal. And the weekly cold set now gets played against loops as standard: repertoire maintenance and grid practice in the same ten minutes.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: subdivision - the secret engine of steady</summary>

Players who feel "locked" aren't tracking beats - they're tracking the layer underneath: eighth notes (the "and"s) or sixteenths, running like a conveyor belt under everything. That's why palm-muted eighth-note chugging (Song Two!) tightens a band instantly - the guitar *becomes* the conveyor belt - and why the pros' trick for any hard rhythm is to count the subdivision out loud ("1-and-2-and...") until the belt runs itself. It's also the real fix for the gap click: you don't bridge silence by feeling the beats, you bridge it by keeping the eighth-note belt running and letting beats fall where they land. When module 6 arrives, this becomes the duo's division of labour: her kick and snare mark the beats, your right hand supplies the belt - which is why guitar-and-drums duos can sound so much bigger than two instruments.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - a week on the grid

1. Run the recording test on Song Two's riff; file the recording and one sentence of diagnosis (eager / sleepy / wander / locked, and where).
2. Add ten grid-game minutes to each session this week: Landing lights and Rush hunt on day one, Gap click and Half-time on day two, player's choice after. Log which game found something.
3. Play this week's cold set against drum loops (find each song's tempo from its chart - they're stamped). Note any song whose feel *changes* against the loop - that's where solo drift was living.
4. End of week: repeat the recording test, same riff, same tempo. Compare at half speed against day one's file. The delta after one honest week is usually audible and always motivating.
5. Optional recruitment: show the drummer the gap-click game on her practice pad app. It's genuinely fun, it's *her* skill too, and it plants the module-6 seed that timing games are things this family does.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if the games feel discouraging</summary>

Lower the tempo 15%, not the standards - honest and slower beats delusional at speed. And remember the reframe: finding drift IS the exercise working (a week ago the same drift existed unheard). The recording test exists precisely so week's end can prove movement.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Two dated recording-test files with audibly tighter placement, rush points named on at least one chart, the cold set running against loops as the new normal, and (ideally) one drummer who thinks the gap game is hers now.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Why does the module use drum loops as the default grid instead of a bare click, and what's the one job the click keeps?
2. Your Rush-hunt recording shows perfect verses but early chorus entries, every time. Which module-1 concept is this the timing version of, and what's the drill?
3. From the deep dive: what are you actually keeping steady during the gap click's silent bar?
4. Predict module 6: her beginner beat will wobble - sometimes slowing across a verse. What does this lesson say your job is when the grid itself moves?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Ears lock to groove (snare) far more naturally than to beeps - loops train musical time. The click's remaining job: half-time ears, where its very sparseness is the exercise.
2. It's a join, in time instead of fingers - and it gets the same treatment: isolate the 4 bars around the entry, loop them with the drum loop until the gear-change bores you.
3. The subdivision belt - eighth notes running internally - not the beats themselves; beats are what the belt drops you onto when sound returns.
4. Follow her. The drummer is the grid, even when the grid wobbles - a band that's together-and-drifting sounds infinitely better than one where the guitarist is "right" and alone. (Module 6 makes this official policy.)

</details>
