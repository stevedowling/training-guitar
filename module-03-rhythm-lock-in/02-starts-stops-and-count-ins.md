# 2 · Starts, stops, and count-ins - the glue of playing together

> **You'll learn:** the small ceremonial skills - counting in, starting as one, ending on purpose - that make two players sound like a band instead of a coincidence.

## Why this matters

Here's what actually goes wrong when two people play together for the first time: not the middle of the song - the *edges*. Nobody knows when to start, so it begins as a ragged stagger; nobody agreed how to end, so it dies apologetically. The middles mostly take care of themselves (you both know the song). Bands sound like bands because the edges are rehearsed - and edge skills are cheap, fast to learn, and almost comically neglected by solo players, who never need them.

## The big picture

The count-in is a tiny protocol carrying everything both players need:

```text
"ONE - TWO - THREE - FOUR - [song]"
  │       │           │
  │       │           └── last two counts: everyone breathes, hands ready
  │       └── the counts ARE the tempo - whatever speed you count is the speed you get
  └── loud, confident, rhythmic - a mumbled count-in is a question, not an instruction
```

The whole skill: **count at the tempo you intend** (harder than it sounds when excited - more below), and make beats 3-4 sacred (no talking, no adjusting the strap, hands landing). For songs with pickup notes or instrument-first intros, the count still works: "1-2-3-4, riff starts on the next 1".

## Counting in at the right tempo

The classic failure: the song lives at 117 bpm, excitement counts it in at 135, and now you're both committed to a version 15% harder than anything you rehearsed. Three fixes, in order of professionalism:

1. **Hum first, count second.** Silently hum the chorus (the section whose tempo your body knows best), *find* the pulse, then count out loud on that pulse.
2. **The tempo is on the chart.** It's been there since module 1 - glance, tap two bars on your leg with a metronome app's silent flash if needed, then count.
3. **Practice count-ins as a drill** - it sounds absurd and works completely: ten count-ins into the first bar of each finished song, checked against the loop. Ten minutes, once, permanent skill.

## Stops and endings: silence on purpose

**Mid-song stops** (Should I Stay trained this solo; now it's social): the rule for two players is *the subdivision belt never stops, even when the sound does* - both players count the silent bar with the same internal eighth notes, and re-entry is together because the belt was shared. Practice: loop the 4 bars around a stop, count the silence out loud together first, then internally.

**Endings** come in three rock flavours, and choosing one *in advance* is the entire skill:

| Ending | How it works | Agree on |
|---|---|---|
| The big chord | last bar hits one held chord, let ring, done | which beat it lands on; who cuts it off (a nod) |
| The button | tight final hit, short - "bop!" | the count: "ending is ON the 4" |
| The rideout | loop the riff, fade energy down, eye contact, together on a final hit | the signal (a look + a lifted neck is the universal one) |

The visual cue deserves its sentence: bands end together because someone *shows* the ending - a lifted guitar neck, an exaggerated final strum wind-up, a nod on the last cycle. Deciding "watch me at the end" out loud feels silly in a living room and is exactly what the pros do on arena stages.

> [!TIP]
> Write the edges on the chart: `COUNT: 117, hum chorus first · END: button on 4, watch me`. Charts have carried structure and tempo; now they carry the social contract too. When the drummer joins, the chart is the agreement.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: why edges matter more than middles (a confidence argument)</summary>

An audience - grandparents, campfire, anyone - forms its judgment of "do these people know what they're doing?" almost entirely at the edges: a confident count and a clean together-start reads as *band* before a single riff lands; a deliberate ending with eye contact and a simultaneous final hit earns applause almost regardless of what happened in verse two (where, as module 1 taught, played-through flubs are barely registered). This is wonderful news for a new duo: edge skills are learnable in a week and don't require virtuosity, so a 9-year-old drummer and her dad can *sound like a band* long before they're an experienced one. Confidence compounds too - a clean start settles both players' nerves, which improves the middle it launches. Rehearse edges first; they're the highest-leverage minutes in ensemble playing.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - edge week

All of it solo-practicable now, all of it reused with the drummer in module 6:

1. **The count-in drill**: ten count-ins per finished song, hum-first method, into bar one with the drum loop - does the loop agree with your count? Log the worst offender song (everyone has one that excitement speeds up).
2. **Choose and chart endings** for all three finished songs - one of each flavour if they fit (Song Two is a natural button; Seven Nation Army is a born big-chord; the third takes the rideout for practice's sake). Add the `COUNT:` and `END:` lines to each chart.
3. **Rehearse each ending** in isolation: last 8 bars + the ending, five times each, treating the visual cue as real even alone (lift the neck at your reflection; feel ridiculous; do it anyway - module 6 needs the habit pre-installed).
4. **Stop drill, socialized**: the 2-bar mute drill from module 2, but now count the silence *out loud* at performance volume. Out-loud counting in front of another person is a skill with its own embarrassment threshold; clear it privately first.
5. This week's cold set: every song starts from a real count-in and lands its charted ending. The set is becoming a performance rehearsal - which was always its destiny.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if count-ins keep racing</summary>

Two bars of the chorus hummed *with a metronome app visible* before counting - eyes on the flash, voice on the pulse - retrains the excitement reflex in a few days. Racing count-ins are near-universal and completely fixable; drummers deal with this in week one of band class (yours will enjoy knowing you had to drill it too).

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Three charts wearing their full social contract (COUNT and END lines), ten-for-ten count-ins at charted tempo on at least two songs, each ending executable with its visual cue, and a cold set that starts and finishes like a band is present - because next module, one is.

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. What two pieces of information does a count-in transmit, and which one do excited players corrupt?
2. Your duo reaches the final bar with no ending agreed. Describe what happens (you've heard it) - then the one chart line that prevents it.
3. During a two-bar stop, what keeps two players synchronized when there's no sound to follow?
4. From the deep dive: why should a brand-new duo rehearse edges before polishing middles?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. When to start and at what tempo - and it's the tempo that excitement corrupts, committing everyone to a too-fast song. Hum first, then count.
2. The apologetic fade: both players slow down, look at each other, trail off, laugh nervously - death by no plan. The line: `END: button on 4, watch me`.
3. The shared subdivision belt - both players counting the same internal eighth notes through the silence. Sound stops; the grid doesn't.
4. Edges are where audiences form their judgment and where confidence is built or spent - and they're cheap: a week of edge work makes a beginner duo *read* as a band, which buys the morale that middle-polishing runs on.

</details>
