# 3 · First duo sessions - following a beginner drummer

> **You'll learn:** the specific craft of playing with a drummer who's nine and new - which turns out to be the same craft as playing with anyone: the drummer is the boss, and your job is to make the boss sound good.

## Why this matters

Everything so far - grid games, edges, stamina - was training for a partner with feelings. This lesson is the handover: the first actual duo sessions. They will be short, occasionally chaotic, and if run right, the single best thing that ever happened to *both* practice routines - because a kid who "has band practice" is a different creature from a kid told to practice drums, and a guitarist with a drummer has a reason to keep songs finished.

## The big picture

The one rule that decides whether this works, printed large because everything else is a footnote to it:

> **The drummer is the boss of time. Wherever she puts the beat, that's where the beat is. If she slows down, the band slows down - *together*. Your years of experience are spent following, not correcting.**

Why (musically): a band that moves together sounds tight even when it drifts; a band where one member is "right" and the other abandoned sounds broken even when the right one is perfect. Grid work gave you the skill to hear her time precisely - the module-1-lesson-of-the-heart is that you'll use that skill to *join* her, not judge her.

Why (humanly): being followed is intoxicating. A nine-year-old who discovers that her kick drum steers an actual loud guitar will chase that feeling - and that chase is the practice motivation problem, solved from the demand side.

## Session shape: short, songful, hers

| Minutes | What | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 | plug in, count-in drill *together* - she counts | hands her the boss role in the first breath |
| 1-4 | THE SONG, attempt one, never stop | the whole point, front-loaded before attention fades |
| 4-7 | one game (below) | play disguised as practice |
| 7-9 | THE SONG, attempt two | almost always dramatically better - let her hear why |
| 9-10 | playback of attempt two, one specific praise each | recording ritual: evidence, pride, done |

Ten minutes. *Shorter than feels worthwhile* - that's correct; end while it's fun and the next session gets requested rather than negotiated. The song is one from your finished three (Seven Nation Army is the canonical first: her simplest quarter-note beat is genuinely the record's drum part). Her part: whatever steady beat she owns - even just kick-snare quarters. It counts. It's a band.

## The games

Each disguises a real ensemble skill as play:

- **The Train** - groove together; she gradually speeds up or slows down *on purpose*; your job is to stay glued. (Trains her tempo control and your following - the rule, gamified.)
- **Freeze** - either player can stop dead mid-groove; the other must freeze within one beat; a nod restarts on a shared count. (Stops, cues, and giggles - the edge skills from lesson 2, socialized.)
- **Loud/Quiet** - she leads dynamics with her hit strength; you shadow instantly. (Dynamics as a duo skill, and another taste of being followed.)
- **Gap bar** - the gap-click game from lesson 1, live: two bars on, one bar silent-but-counted, back in together. Graduating skill; save it for session three or four.

Rules of all games: she picks which one, thirty seconds of explanation max, and any game that isn't landing gets dropped without comment - the inventory exists so no single game carries the session.

> [!TIP]
> Praise the specific, not the general: "your snare on 2 and 4 was rock solid in the chorus" beats "good job buddy" by a mile - it tells her what to be proud of and repeats the vocabulary. And when *you* flub, say so cheerfully and play on: modelling the never-stop rule out loud ("ha, missed it - catching the next one") teaches the deepest lesson in this course better than any instruction could.

<details>
<summary>🔍 Deep dive: what following a wobbly drummer does to your playing (all of it good)</summary>

Following a beginner's time is elite ear training in disguise. Session players rate "plays well with shaky time-keepers" among the hardest hire-able skills: it demands hearing the *other* part in real detail while executing your own on autopilot - exactly the automation your module-4 singing work needs, arriving from another direction. It also forces radical simplification: when the beat wobbles, busy guitar parts collapse, so you'll instinctively strip to the chug, the anchor strums, the spine of the song - and discovering the spine of each song is *arranging*, module 6's entire subject, learned by necessity. There's a name for the stance you're practicing: accompaniment - the art of making the other player sound better than they are. It's the most employable skill in music and the best parenting disguise ever devised.

</details>

## 🛠️ Try it - the first three sessions

1. **Recruit properly**: not "want to practice together?" but "I need a drummer - I've got a song." Show her the finished Song One recording. Let the band have thirty seconds of name-brainstorming (bands with names rehearse; it's science of the unfalsifiable kind).
2. **Session one**: the shape above, Seven Nation Army, her steadiest beat, she counts in (coach the hum-first trick if she races - she will; everyone does; you did). Record attempt two. Expect ragged; celebrate *together-ragged*.
3. **Sessions two and three** across the next week or two, whenever her energy allows: same song until the band requests otherwise, one new game per session, playback every time. Track nothing except attendance and one specific-praise note in your log.
4. **Your homework between sessions**: note where following her was hardest (usually: her verse tempo sags then chorus surges - the Train game targets exactly this) and simplify your part wherever you couldn't listen and play at once. Simpler and locked beats fancy and alone - write the simplifications on the chart; module 6 formalizes them.
5. After session three, one question to her, verbatim, logged: *"what song should the band do next?"* Her answer is module 6's setlist seed - and the moment the band becomes partly hers.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint - if a session goes sideways</summary>

Sideways is normal (she's nine; some days the kit is a drum solo delivery device). The recovery move is always the same: shrink, don't push - drop to just the game, or just one chorus, or just playback of last time's recording, and end warm. A two-minute session that ends laughing beats a ten-minute one that ends negotiated. The streak logic from module 1 applies to the band too: frequency builds the identity; duration can wait.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ What done looks like</summary>

Three short duo sessions happened and were *asked about afterwards*; a recording exists of attempt-two-session-three that is recognisably the song; your chart carries simplification notes; the band possibly has a terrible name; and there's an answer on file to "what song next?". Module 3 complete - the electric track pauses for [Module 6 · The Family Band](../module-06-the-family-band/) (or runs parallel with the campfire track - the map allows both).

</details>

## ✋ Checkpoint

1. Mid-song her tempo sags noticeably through the verse. List what you do and what you absolutely don't - and the game that works on this between songs.
2. Why does the session put the full song at minute one instead of warming up with exercises?
3. From the deep dive: name two skills following a beginner drummer trains in *you*, and which later modules cash them.
4. She nails a session and you say "good job!". Upgrade the praise using this lesson's rule - then explain why the upgrade matters beyond niceness.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Do: sag with her, stay glued, keep the belt running under the new tempo. Don't: push the "correct" tempo, stop, or coach mid-song. Between songs: the Train, where tempo-moving is the game and she drives.
2. Attention economics of a 9-year-old (front-load the payoff) and band identity (a session that *is* a song rehearsal, not exercises, is what makes her a drummer in a band rather than a kid doing drills).
3. Hearing another part while playing on autopilot (module 4's automation, from another angle) and stripping songs to their spine (module 6's arranging). Accompaniment stance underlies both.
4. "Your snare on 2 and 4 held the chorus together" - specific praise tells her what skill to own and repeat, builds shared vocabulary, and makes the praise credible (kids audit compliments harder than adults do).

</details>
